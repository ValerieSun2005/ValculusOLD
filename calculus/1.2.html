1.2 — Limit Properties

This section covers limit properties involving the sums, differences, products, quotients, and compositions of functions. It also covers the laws and procedures for more-complex types of limits.

We must begin by introducing trivial limit properties, which enable us to prove later limit laws. Consider a constant \(c.\) We first have \[\lim_{x \to a} c = c\,,\] which is intuitive because if we let \(f(x) = c,\) a continuous function, then it follows that \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) = c.\) We also have the property that \[\lim_{x \to a} x = a\,.\] Additionally, if we have the function \(cf(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists, then \[\lim_{x \to a} [cf(x)] = c \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\,.\] These limit laws are all intuitive since they are made palpable by drawing the graphs of \(y = c,\) \(y = x,\) and \(y = cf(x),\) respectively. You may find a proof to all the properties in this section in this PDF .

Let us now discuss the limits of sums, differences, products, and quotients of functions. Suppose that we have two functions \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) such that \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both exist. Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)/g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x)/ \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,, \, \textrm{given that} \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \ne 0\,. \\[2ex] \ea \]

LIMITS OF SUMS, DIFFERENCES, PRODUCTS, AND QUOTIENTS
If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both exist, then

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \,.\) \((1)\)

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,.\) \((2)\)

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,.\) \((3)\)

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)/g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) / \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,.\) \((4)\)

EXAMPLE \(1\)
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be functions such that \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4\) and \(\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = -2.\) Calculate the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) + g(x)]\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x) - g(x)]\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x) g(x)]\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x)/g(x)]\)
  1. The limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) both exist, so we can apply property \((1)\) to obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \\ &= 4 - 2 \\ &= \boxed{2\,.} \ea \]
  2. We can apply property \((2)\) to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \\ &= 4 - (-2) \\ &= \boxed{6\,.} \ea \]
  3. The application of property \((3)\) gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \\ &= 4 \cdot (-2) \\ &= \boxed{-8\,.} \ea \]
  4. Using property \((4),\) we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to 2} g(x)} \\[1ex] &= \frac{4}{-2} \\[1ex] &= \boxed{-2\,.} \ea \]

If \(n\) is a constant and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists, then we have the following laws with exponents: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a} x^n &= a^n \,. \\ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{x} &= \sqrt[n]{a}\,.\\ \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n &= \left[\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\right]^n\,. \\ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} &= \sqrt[n]{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}\,. \ea \]

LIMIT PROPERTIES WITH EXPONENTS
If \(n\) is a constant and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists, then

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} x^n = a^n\,.\) \((5)\)

\(\ds \hspace{-0.15ex} \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{x} = \sqrt[n]{a}\,.\) \((6)\)

\(\ds \hspace{4.6ex} \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n = \left[\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\right]^n\,.\) \((7)\)

\(\ds \hspace{3ex} \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}\,.\) \((8)\)

EXAMPLE \(2\)
Consider Figure 1, in which the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) are shown. Compute the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -1} [f(x) + g(x)]\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \, 0 \;} [f(x) - g(x)]\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \, 0 \;} [f(x) + g(x)]\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x)/g(x)]\)
  1. We see that \(\lim_{x \to -1} f(x) = 1\) and \(\lim_{x \to -1} g(x) = 2.\) Thus, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -1} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to -1} f(x) + \lim_{x \to -1} g(x) \\ &= 1 + 2 \\ &= \boxed{3\,.} \ea \]
  2. We see that neither \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) nor \(\lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\) exists, so we cannot use property \((2).\) However, we can apply property \((2)\) for the one-sided limits because each one exists: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) \\ &= 3 - 2 \\ &= 1\,; \\ \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) \\ &= 4 - 3 \\ &= 1\,. \ea \] Since these one-sided limits are equal, we have \[\lim_{x \to 0} [f(x) - g(x)] = \boxed{1\,.}\]
  3. Similar to part (ii), neither \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) nor \(\lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\) exists. We cannot apply property \((1),\) but we can apply it to the one-sided limits of the sum: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) \\ &= 3 + 2 \\ &= 5\,; \\ \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) \\ &= 4 + 3 \\ &= 7\,. \ea \] Because these one-sided limits are not equivalent, the limit does not exist.
  4. In this case, we can apply property \((4)\) to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{\lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x)} \\[2ex] &= \boxed{4/3\,.} \ea \]

For limit properties \((1)\) through \((4),\) the nonexistence of \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and/or \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) does NOT imply that the limit of the sum, difference, product, or quotient does not exist. These properties are theorems that can be applied to some limits. For example, consider the function \(k(x) = \sin(x)/x.\) You may verify using a graphing calculator that \(\lim_{x \to 0} k(x) = 1,\) but the fallacious application of property \((3)\) could produce \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \sin x \cdot \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x}\, , \ea \] in which \(\lim_{x \to 0} (1/x)\) does not exist. Moreover, the one-sided limits of \(1/x\) as \(x \to 0^-\) and \(x \to 0^+\) also do not exist, so the application of property \((3)\) is not possible.

Let us now discuss the strategy for computing limits of composite functions. Consider the general composition of two functions \(f(g(x))\) \(\big[\)also written as \((f \circ g)(x)\big].\) If \(f\) is continuous at the value \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x),\) then direct substitution provides

\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(g(x)) = f \left(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) \right)\,.\) \((9)\)

This fact is a generalization that relates properties \((5)\) through \((8).\) Problems become more complicated if \(f\) is undefined or discontinuous at \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x),\) requiring special thought because property \((9)\) does not apply. Similar to the note on properties \((1)\) through \((4),\) you may not conclude that a limit of a composite function does not exist if property \((9)\) cannot be used.

EXAMPLE \(3\)
Calculate the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} \sqrt{4x + 8}\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} 2^{3x - x^2}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \cos^2(4x)\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 8e} \ln(x - 4e)\)
  1. \(\sqrt{4x + 8}\) is continuous at all \(x\) in its domain, so at \(x = 2\) we have, using property \(8,\) \[\lim_{x \to 2} \sqrt{4x + 8} = \sqrt{4(2) + 8} = \boxed{4\,.}\]
  2. Since \(2^{3x - x^2}\) is continuous at \(x = 1,\) we obtain, by property \((7),\) \[\lim_{x \to 1} 2^{3x - x^2} = 2^{3(1) - (1)^2} = \boxed{4\,.}\]
  3. Cosine is a continuous function; thus, its square is also continuous. Using properties \((7)\) and \((9)\) then gives \[\lim_{x \to \pi/2} \cos^2(4x) = \cos^2(4 \pi / 2) = \boxed{1\,.}\]
  4. The application of property \((9)\) provides \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 8e} \ln(x - 4e) &= \ln(8e - 4e) \\ &= \ln(4e) \\ &= \boxed{1 + \ln 4\,.} \ea \]
EXAMPLE \(4\)
Consider Figure 2, in which the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) are shown. Compute the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2^-} g(f(x))\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} f(g(x)) \)
  3. \(\ds \hspace{-0.65ex} \lim_{x \to -1} g(f(x) - 1)\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} g(x^2 - 3)\)
  1. The first step of evaluating the limit of a composite function is to determine whether property \((9)\) can be used. That is, we need to determine whether \(g(x)\) is continuous at the value \(x = \lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x),\) assuming this limit exists. From the graph, we have \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) = -4,\) and \(g\) is continuous at \(x = -4.\) Thus, we can apply property \((9) \!:\) \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2^-} g(f(x)) &= g\left(\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x)\right) \\ &= g(-4) \\ &= \boxed{-2\,.} \ea \]
  2. From the graph, we see that \(\lim_{x \to 1} g(x) = 2,\) but \(f\) is not continuous at this value because exists a jump discontinuity at that point. Therefore, we cannot apply property \((9)\) to this limit. However, we notice that \(g(x) \to 2\) solely from below as \(x \to 1;\) in other words, near \(x = 1,\) \(g\) only takes on values less than \(2.\) Thus, \[\lim_{x \to 1} f(g(x)) = \lim_{g(x) \to 2} f(g(x))\,,\] or, equivalently, \[\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) = \boxed{-4 \,.}\] Since \(g\) never takes on values greater than \(2\) as \(x \to 1,\) we do not consider the one-sided limit \(\lim_{x \to 2^+} f(x).\) These values of \(g,\) which are less than \(2,\) are inputted into the function \(f\) and therefore correspond to the left-hand limit, \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x).\)
  3. As \(x \to -1,\) \((f(x) - 1) \to 3.\) However, \(g\) is not continuous at \(x = 3,\) so we cannot apply property \((9).\) As \(x \to -1,\) \((f(x) - 1)\) approaches \(3\) from the top and bottom: it takes on values both less than \(3\) and greater than \(3.\) Thus, we cannot consider a single one-sided limit as we did in part (ii), so we must write \[ \lim_{x \to -1} g(f(x) - 1) = \lim_{x \to 3} g(x)\,, \] which does not exist.
  4. As \(x \to 0,\) \((x^2 - 3) \to -3.\) We may NOT use property \((9)\) to conclude that \[\lim_{x \to 0} g(x^2 - 3) = g \left(\lim_{x \to 0} (x^2 - 3) \right) = g(3)\, ,\] however, because \(g\) is not continuous at \(x = 3.\) Instead, we notice that as \(x \to 0,\) \((x^2 - 3) \to -3\) from ABOVE only (i.e., from values greater than \(-3\)). Therefore, we may consider only the right-hand limit of \(g \!:\) \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} g(x^2 - 3) &= \lim_{x \to -3^+} g(x) \\ &= \boxed{3\,.} \ea \]

SECTION SUMMARY
  • If \(c\) is a constant, then \[\lim_{x \to a} c= \lim_{x \to a} c\,.\]
  • If \(c\) is a constant and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists, then \[\lim_{x \to a} [cf(x)] = c \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\,.\]
  • \[\lim_{x \to a} x = a\,.\]
  • If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both exist, then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\,. \\[2ex] \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)/g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x)/ \lim_{x \to a} g(x), \, \textrm{given that} \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \ne 0 \,. \\[2ex] \ea \]
  • If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and/or \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) do not exist, then you may NOT assume that the limit of a sum, difference, product, or quotient of \(f\) and \(g\) does not exist. In some situations, you may apply properties \((1)\) through \((4)\) to the one-sided limits of \(f\) and \(g,\) while in others the properties are not applicable.
  • If \(n\) is a constant and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists, then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a} x^n &= a^n \\ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{x} &= \sqrt[n]{a}\\ \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n &= \left[\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\right]^n \\ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} &= \sqrt[n]{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}\,. \ea \]
  • If \(f\) is continuous at the value \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x),\) then \[\lim_{x \to a} f(g(x)) = f \left(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) \right)\,.\] Remember that if you cannot use this property, you may NOT conclude that \(\lim_{x \to a} f(g(x))\) does not exist.
