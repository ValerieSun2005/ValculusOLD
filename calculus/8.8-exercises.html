8.8 Exercises — The Cross Product

For exercises 1–4, find the vector components of the following cross products.

(1) \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{A} = \left\langle -1, 4, -9 \right\rangle, \vec{B} = \left\langle 2, -7, -8 \right\rangle \).

(2) \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{u} = \left\langle 2, -8, 13 \right\rangle, \vec{v} = \left\langle -7, 1, 0 \right\rangle \).

(3) \(2 \left(\vec{b} \times \vec{a} \right), \textrm{where} \, \vec{a} = \left\langle 8, -1, 5 \right\rangle, \vec{b} = \left\langle 6, -3, 27 \right\rangle \).

(4) \(-10 \left( \vec{p} \times \vec{q} \right) , \textrm{where} \, \vec{p} = \left\langle 0, -1, 5 \right\rangle, \vec{q} = \left\langle 1, -3, 0 \right\rangle \).

For exercises 5–8, find the magnitude of the following vectors.

(5) \(\left|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\right|, \textrm{where} \, \vec{a} = \left\langle 12, 3, 5 \right\rangle, \vec{b} = \left\langle 7, -13, 5 \right\rangle \).

(6) \(\left|\vec{P} \times \vec{Q}\right|, \textrm{where} \, \vec{P} = \left\langle -22, 0, 2 \right\rangle, \vec{Q} = \left\langle -1, 13, 5 \right\rangle \).

(7) \(\frac{16}{5} \left|\vec{B} \times \vec{A}\right|, \textrm{where} \, \vec{A} = \left\langle 3, 15, -17 \right\rangle, \vec{B} = \left\langle 14, 26, -4 \right\rangle\).

(8) \(3 \left|\vec{C} \times \vec{D}\right|, \textrm{where} \, \vec{C} = \left\langle 13, -22, 0 \right\rangle, \vec{D} = \left\langle -12, 0, -9 \right\rangle \).

Exercises 9–14 require you to use cross products to find the area of geometric objects.

(9) \(\Delta ABC\) has vertices \(A(1,3)\), \(B(2,-4)\), and \(C(6,8)\). Find the area of \(\Delta ABC\).

(10) \(\Delta DEF\) has vertices \(D(1,3)\), \(E(2,-4)\), and \(F(6,8)\). Find the area of \(\Delta DEF\).

(11) \(\unicode{x25B1} PQRS\) has verticles \(P(-3, 4)\), \(Q(2, 5)\), \(R(4, -1)\), and \(S(-1,-2)\). Find the area of \(\unicode{x25B1} PQRS\).

(12) \(\unicode{x25B1} ABCD\) has vertices \(A(-2,4)\), \(B(4,2)\), \(C(-2,0)\), and \(D(4,-2)\). Find the area of \(\unicode{x25B1} ABCD\).

(13) An isosceles trapezoid has vertices located at the origin, \(A(3, 2)\), \(B(5,2)\), and \(C(8,0)\).

(14) A regular hexagon, centered about the origin, has a vertex at the point \((2, 2)\). Find the area of the hexagon.

Exercises 15–22 are exercises based on the applications for this section.

(15) Torque measures the ability of a force to cause rotational acceleration to an object. Torque, \(\tau\), is defined as \(\vec{r} \times \vec{F}\), where \(r\) denotes the distance from the center of rotation to the point where the force \(F\) is applied. A door of length \(0.9\) meter is opened from the doorknob at an \(85\) degree angle with a force of \(3\) newtons. What is the torque on the door as it is opened?

(16) A person of mass \(m\) sits at the end of a seesaw of length \(R\). The person's weight, \(mg\) (\(g\) is the constant of gravitational acceleration, equal to \(9.8\) meters per second squared), exerts a torque on the seesaw. By Newton's Second Law for rotation, \(\tau = I \alpha\), where \(\tau\) is the net torque; \(I\) is the moment of inertia of the seesaw, defined as \(I = mR^2\); and \(\alpha\) is the angular acceleration of the seesaw. Neglecting the friction of the fulcrum, derive an expression for the angular acceleration of the seesaw in terms of \( R\) and the physical constant \(g\).

(17) Angular momentum is defined as \(\vec{r} \times m\vec{v}\), where \(m\) denotes the mass of an object, \(v\) denotes its velocity, and \(r\) denotes the distance from the object to the center of rotation. A \(4\) kilogram object travels at a constant velocity of \(0.8\) meter per second at a distance \(1.2\) meters from the center of rotation. What is the object's linear momentum \(\vec{L}\)?
