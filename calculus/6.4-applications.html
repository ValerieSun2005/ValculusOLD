5.5, 5.6, 5.7 — Applications

In my opinion, the applications for Solids of Revolution are the most fun! Usually, the concepts are taught but never applied. I've merged sections 5.5, 5.6, and 5.7 together here because of their unity. In this section we'll be discussing applications ranging from fun math projects to derivations of everyday formulas.

APPLICATION 1 — Volume of a Sphere

For this application we'll derive the volume of a sphere by using the Disk Method. We know that a semi-circle is in the form \(\sqrt{r^2 - x^2}\), where \(r\) is the radius.

Notice that if we rotate the region bounded by the semi-circle and the \(x\)-axis about the \(x\)-axis, we obtain a sphere. To find the expression for its volume, we can use the Disk Method — construct and integrate infinitely many disks of length \(dx\).

In this case \(r(x) = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}\), and our bounds of integration are from \(-r\) to \(r\). Therefore, the volume is:

$$ \pi \int_{-r}^{r} r(x)^2 \, dx = \pi \int_{-r}^{r} (r^2 - x^2) \,dx $$

Note that because our function is even(symmetric about the \(y\)-axis), we can rewrite this as:

$$ 2\pi \int_{0}^{r} (r^2 - x^2) \, dx $$

Now integrating:

$$ 2\pi \int_{0}^{r} (r^2 - x^2) \, dx = 2\pi \Big[xr^2 - \frac{x^3}{3}\Big]_0^r $$ $$ = 2\pi\Big[\frac{2r^3}{3} - 0\Big] $$ $$ = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 $$

And this is the formula we recognize as the volume of a sphere!
