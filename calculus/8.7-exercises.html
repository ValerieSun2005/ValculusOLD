8.7 Exercises — The Dot Product

A calculator is required for the following problems.

For exercises 1–9, evaluate the following dot products.

(1) \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{A} = 2 \boldsymbol{i} + 3 \boldsymbol{j}, \vec{B} = 4 \boldsymbol{i} -1 \boldsymbol{j}\).

(2) \(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{a} = \left\langle 6, 1 \right\rangle, \vec{b} = \left\langle 0, 2 \right\rangle\).

(3) \(\vec{R} \cdot \vec{S}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{R} = -4 \boldsymbol{i} + 7 \boldsymbol{j}, \vec{S} = -2 \boldsymbol{i} + 5 \boldsymbol{j}\).

(4) \(\vec{B} \cdot \vec{C}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{B} = \left\langle 1, 0, -11 \right\rangle, \vec{C} = \left\langle 12, -7, -8 \right\rangle\).

(5) \(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{a} = \left\langle 9, -8, 2 \right\rangle, \vec{C} = \left\langle -7, 1, 3 \right\rangle\).

(6) \(\vec{p} \cdot \vec{q}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{p} = -5 \boldsymbol{i} + 3 \boldsymbol{j} + 4 \boldsymbol{k}, \vec{q} = 8 \boldsymbol{i} + 12 \boldsymbol{j} + 1 \boldsymbol{k}\).

(7) \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{A} = \left\langle 3, 11, -1, 5 \right\rangle, \vec{B} = \left\langle 0, 9, 2, 15 \right\rangle\).

(8) \(\vec{V} \cdot \vec{U}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{U} = \left\langle -8, 0, 22, -4 \right\rangle, \vec{V} = \left\langle 1, -4, -13, 19 \right\rangle\).

(9) \(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}, \textrm{where} \, \vec{b} = \left\langle -2, 5, 18, -7, 9 \right\rangle, \vec{a} = \left\langle -3, 15, -8, 7, -10 \right\rangle\).

For exercises 10–16, find the angle between the following vectors.

(10) \(\vec{A} = \left\langle 2, 9 \right\rangle, \vec{B} = \left\langle -3, 7 \right\rangle \).

(11) \(\vec{p} = 12 \boldsymbol{i} -5 \boldsymbol{j}, \vec{q} = 7 \boldsymbol{i} + 0 \boldsymbol{j} \).

(12) \(\vec{u} = 4 \boldsymbol{i} + 1 \boldsymbol{j} , \vec{v} = 13 \boldsymbol{i} -8 \boldsymbol{j} \).

(13) \(\vec{C} = \left\langle -3, 7, 12 \right\rangle, \vec{D} = \left\langle -6, 4, 0 \right\rangle \).

(14) \(\vec{b} = \left\langle -8, -2, 3 \right\rangle, \vec{c} = \left\langle 5, 19, -1 \right\rangle \).

(15) \(\vec{A} = 11 \boldsymbol{i} -9 \boldsymbol{j} + 5 \boldsymbol{k}, \vec{B} = -20 \boldsymbol{i} + 3 \boldsymbol{j} + 6 \boldsymbol{k} \).

(16) \(\vec{b} = \left\langle 8, 4, -12, 3 \right\rangle, \vec{c} = \left\langle -2, 1, -18, 34 \right\rangle \).

Exercises 17–23 are conceptual questions.

(17) Suppose that \(\theta\) is the angle between two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). Explain why \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\) is positive when \(\theta \lt 90°\), negative when \(\theta \gt 90°\), and \(0\) when \(\theta = 90°\).

(18) Explain why the dot product operation is commutative.

(19) At the supermarket, pumpkins cost \($5\), watermelons cost \($4.50\), and apples cost \($1.99\) per pound. These prices are arranged in the vector price \(= \left\langle 5, 4.50, 1.99 \right\rangle\). In a typical hour, there are \(36\) pumpkins sold, \(45\) watermelons sold, and \(3.8\) pounds of apples sold. These sales are arranged in the vector sales \(= \left\langle 36, 45, 3.8 \right\rangle\). Evaluate and interpret price \(\cdot\) sales.

(20) Oreos come in packs of \(3, 6,\) and \(45\), as represented by the vector quantity \( = \left\langle 3, 6, 45 \right\rangle\). In a pantry, there are \(1\) pack of \(3\) oreos, \(4\) packs of \(6\) oreos, and \(2\) packages of \(45\) oreos, as represented by the vector pantry \(= \left\langle 1, 4, 2 \right\rangle\). Evaluate and intepret quantity \(\cdot\) pantry.

(21) Is a dot product defined for three vectors? What about for five vectors?

(22) Explain why the expression \(\vec{c}\cdot \left(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\right)\) is meaningless.

(23) Let \(\vec{A} = \left\langle a_x, a_y, a_z\right\rangle\), and let \(\vec{B} = \left\langle b_x, b_y, b_z \right\rangle\). Describe what happens to the angle between the two vectors if \(a_x + b_x = \left| \vec{A} \right| + \left| \vec{B}\right|\).

Exercises 24–28 are analytic exercises.

(24) Two vectors, \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), subtend an acute angle \(\theta\). Suppose that the components of \(\vec{A}\) are \(\left\langle a_1, a_2 \right\rangle\). What happens to \(\theta\) if the components are transformed such that \(\left(\vec{A}\right)' = \left\langle -a_2, a_1 \right\rangle\)?

(25) Let \(\vec{p} = \beta \boldsymbol{i} - 3\boldsymbol{j}\), and let \(\vec{q} = 16 \boldsymbol{i} + 1.4 \boldsymbol{j}\). Find the value of \(\beta\) for which the angle between \(\vec{p}\) and \(\vec{q}\) equals \(50°\).

(26) Vector \(\vec{A} = \left\langle \alpha, -2 \right\rangle\) is perpendicular to vector \(\vec{B} = \left\langle -5, 14 \right\rangle\). Find the value of \(\alpha\).

(27) Suppose that two vectors, \(\vec{R} = 3 \boldsymbol{i} - m \boldsymbol{j}\) and \(\vec{S} = 2 \boldsymbol{i} + 8 \boldsymbol{j}\), are parallel to each other. Find a value of \(m\).

(28) Vectors \(\vec{A} = \left\langle 3, k \right\rangle\) and \(\vec{B} = \left\langle -1, 3 \right\rangle\) subtend an angle of \(45.6°\). Find the value of \(k\).

(29) Two vectors, \(\vec{E} = \left\langle 3, -6, p \right\rangle\) and \(\vec{F} = \left\langle 12, 1, q \right\rangle\), subtend an angle of \(68.7°\). \(p\) and \(q\) are constants such that \(pq = 15\). What is \(p + q\) ?

(30) Let \(\vec{a} = 4\boldsymbol{i} + \rho \boldsymbol{j}\), and let \(\vec{b} = 2\boldsymbol{i} + 2 \boldsymbol{j}\), where \(\rho\) is a constant. Find \(\lim_{\rho \to \infty} \theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\).

(31) The vector-valued functions \(\vec{u}(t) = \left\langle \cos(t), t^3 - 1 \right\rangle\) and \(\vec{v}(t) = \left\langle \sin(t), t - 4 \right\rangle\) will be multiplied using the dot product operation. Find the values of \(t\), \(0 \lt t \lt 4\), for which \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0\).

Exercises 32–42 are application-based questions.
