Problem 34 — 5.1 Exercises (Integration by Parts)

Consider the family of integrals \(\displaystyle \int x^p e^x \, \dd x,\) where \(p\) is a positive constant. Show that integration by parts successfully yields an antiderivative for \(p = 1, 2, 3, \dots\) but not for \(\displaystyle p = \frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}, \frac{5}{2}, \dots.\)

SOLUTION In order for integration by parts to successfully return an antiderivative for \(\displaystyle \int x^p e^x \, \dd x,\) where \(p\) is a positive integer, we can perform tabular integration by differentiating \(x^p\) and integrating \(e^x.\)


\(D\) \(I\)
\(+\) \(x^p\) \(e^x\)
\(-\) \(px^{p - 1}\) \(e^x\)
\(+\) \(p(p - 1)x^{p - 2}\) \(e^x\)
\(\pm\) \(\cdots\) \(\cdots\)
\(\pm\) \(p(p - 1)(p - 2)(p - 3) \cdot \, \cdots\) \(e^x\)
\(\mp\) \(0\) \(e^x\)

If \(p\) is a positive integer, then the continued differentiation of \(x^p\) will eventually yield \(0.\) Therefore, drawing arrows and combining the terms give \[ \int x^p e^x \, \dd x = x^p e^x - px^{p - 1} e^x + p(p - 1)x^{p - 2} e^x + \cdots + p(p - 1)(p - 2) \cdot \, \cdots \, \cdot e^x + C.\] However, the continued differentiation of \(x^p,\) where \(\displaystyle p = \frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}, \frac{5}{2}, \dots , \) will never reach zero.
