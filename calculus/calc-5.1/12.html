Problem 12 — 5.1 Exercises (Integration by Parts)

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \sin(\alpha) \cos(\alpha) \ln(\sec \alpha ) \, \dd \alpha. \)

SOLUTION Using properties of logarithms, we know that \[\ln(\sec \alpha) = \ln(\cos(\alpha)^{-1}) = - \ln(\cos \alpha).\] Therefore, we can rewrite the integral as \[\int -\sin(\alpha) \cos(\alpha) \ln(\cos \alpha ) \, \dd \alpha. \] At this point, we can perform the substitution \(q = \cos(\alpha).\) Then, \(\dd q = -\sin(\alpha) \, \dd \alpha\) and the integral becomes \[\int q \ln(q) \, \dd q.\] Using the integration by parts formula, \[\int u \, \dd v = uv - \int v \, \dd u,\] we let \(u = \ln(q)\) and \(\dd v = q \, \dd q.\) Then, \(\displaystyle \dd u = \frac{1}{q} \, \dd q\) and \(\displaystyle v = \frac{1}{2} q^2.\) We then obtain \[ \begin{aligned} \int q \ln(q) \, \dd q &= \frac{1}{2} q^2 \ln(q) - \int \left(\frac{1}{2} q^2\right) \left(\frac{1}{q} \, \dd q \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} q^2 \ln(q) - \frac{1}{2} \int q \, \dd q \\ &= \frac{1}{2} q^2 \ln(q) - \frac{1}{4} q^2 + C. \end{aligned} \] Substituting back gives \[ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt] {\frac{1}{2} \cos^2(\alpha) \ln(\cos \alpha) - \frac{1}{4} \cos^2(\alpha) + C. } \]
