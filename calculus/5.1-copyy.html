5.1 — Integration by Parts

Up to this point, we have seen integrals that can be evaluated with a simple substitution, such as \(\int \cos(2x) \, \dd x , \) which can be evaluated using the substitution \(u = 2x\). In this section, however, we will explore a way to rewrite integrals as expressions that are easier to evaluate.

Let \(u\) and \(v\) be two functions of \(x\). The product rule states that the derivative of the product \(uv\) is given by \[(uv)' = u' v + u v'.\] If we integrate both sides, we will be reversing the effect of the product rule. This procedure will be useful because it produces the formula on which we base the integration technique featured in this section. We will get \[ \begin{aligned} \int (uv)' \, \dd x &= \int u' v \, \dd x + \int u v' \, \dd x \\ uv &= \int u' v \, \dd x + \int u v' \, \dd x \\ \int u v' \, \dd x &= uv - \int u' v \, \dd x. \end{aligned} \] We can condense the terms \(v' \, \dd x\) and \(u' \, \dd x\) as \(\dd v\) and \(\dd u\), respectively. Thus, we can rewrite the formula as \[\int u \, \dd v = uv - \int v \, \dd u. \] In words, this formula permits us to re-express an integral \(\int u \, \dd v\) by choosing a term to differentiate (this is our \(u\)) and a term to integrate (this is our \(\dd v\).) The resulting expression, \(uv - \int v \, \dd u,\) will hopefully be easier to evaluate.


INTEGRATION BY PARTS FORMULA

\[\int u \, \dd v = uv - \int v \, \dd u. \]


Consider, as an example, the integral \(\int x e^x \, \dd x.\) A substitution will not work for evaluating the integral, but we can instead try integration by parts. In this case, we can pick \[ u = x \quad \textrm{and} \quad \dd v = e^x \, \dd x. \] To complete the remaining portion of the new expression, we need \(v\) and \(\dd u.\) We can differentiate \(u\) to get \(\dd u\) and integrate \(\dd v\) to get \(v.\) \[ \dd u = 1 \, \dd x \quad \textrm{and} \quad v = e^x. \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int xe^x \, \dd x &= x\left(e^x\right) - \int e^x \left(1 \, \dd x\right) \\ &= xe^x - \int e^x \, \dd x \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{xe^x - e^x + C.} \end{aligned} \] We notice that our choice to let \(u = x\) was correct—the resulting integral, \(\int e^x \, \dd x\), is easily evaluable. However, had we let \(u = e^x\) and \(\dd v = x \, \dd x,\) the resulting integral would have been \(\int \frac{1}{2} x^2 e^x \, \dd x\), which would have lead nowhere. This example highlights the fact that it is not sufficient to simply have the formula memorized. You must continue to practice integration by parts to be able to determine the correct expressions for \(u\) and \(\dd v\).


There is a general mnemonic that can help us decide what to let \(u\), called "LIATE," which is an acronym for "\((1)\) logarithms, \((2)\) inverse trigonometric functions, \((3)\) algebraic expressions, \((4)\) trigonometric functions, and \((5)\) exponential functions." The mnemonic suggests that we prioritize our choice of \(u\) according to the order of those list items.


CHOOSING \(u\) — "LIATE"

Select \(u\) in the following order:

  1. Logarithms; e.g., \(\ln(x)\)
  2. Inverse Trigonometric Functions; e.g., \(\sin^{-1}(x)\)
  3. Algebraic Expressions; e.g., \(x^2\)
  4. Trigonometric Functions; e.g., \(\sin(x)\)
  5. Exponential Functions; e.g., \(e^x\)

It is important to note that the acronym only serves as a general guideline. There are, of course, exceptions to the list, especially when an integrand contains multiple of one item (e.g., multiple algebraic expressions).


EXAMPLE \(1\)
Evaluate the following integrals.
  1. \(\displaystyle \int x \sin(x) \, \dd x\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int x^2 \cos(x) \, \dd x\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \int x^3 \sqrt{9 - x^2} \, \dd x\)
  1. For this problem, we have an algebraic expression, \(x\), and a trigonometric function, \(\sin(x)\). Following the acronym "LIATE," we are instructed to let \(u\) equal the algebraic expression. Thus, let \[u = x \quad \textrm{and} \quad \dd v = \sin(x) \, \dd x.\] Then, \[\dd u = 1 \, \dd x \quad \textrm{and} \quad v = - \cos(x) .\] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int x \sin(x) \, \dd x &= x \left(- \cos(x)\right) - \int (- \cos(x)) (1 \, \dd x) \\ &= - x \cos(x) + \int \cos(x) \, \dd x \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{-x \cos(x) + \sin(x) + C.} \end{aligned} \]

  2. We have an algebraic expression, \(x^2\), and a trigonometric function, \(\cos(x)\). Following "LIATE," the algebraic expression, \(x^2\) in this case, is higher on the list than the trigonometric function is. Therefore, we let \(u = x^2\) and \(\dd v = \cos(x) \, \dd x.\) Then, it follows that \(\dd u = 2x \, \dd x\) and \(v = \sin(x).\) Plugging in these expressions, we get \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int x^2 \cos(x) \, \dd x &= x^2 \sin(x) - \int 2x \sin(x) \, \dd x \\ &= x^2 \sin(x) - 2\int x \sin(x) \, \dd x \\ \end{aligned} \]

    At this point, we will have to do integration by parts again in order to evaluate \(\int x \sin(x) \, \dd x\). Luckily, we have already evaluated it in part I., so we can plug it in to obtain \[ \begin{aligned} &= x^2 \sin(x) - 2\int x \sin(x) \, \dd x \\ &= x^2 \sin(x) - 2(-x \cos(x) + \sin(x)) + C \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{x^2 \sin(x) + 2x \cos(x) - 2 \sin(x) + C.} \end{aligned} \]

  3. In this case, because we have multiple algebraic expressions in the integrand, "LIATE" does not help us. However, if we rewrite the integral as \[\int x^2 \cdot x \sqrt{9 - x^2} \, \dd x, \] we can pick \(u = x^2\) and \(\dd v = x \sqrt{9 - x^2} \, \dd x\). Then, \(\dd u = 2x \, \dd x\) and \(v\) can be evaluated by performing a substitution (we have already used \(u\) and \(v\), so any other variable will work for the substitution—we will choose \(k\).) \[ \begin{aligned} \textrm{Let} \, \, k &= 9 - x^2 \\ \dd k &= -2x \, \dd x. \end{aligned} \] \[ \begin{aligned} v &= \int x \sqrt{9 - x^2} \, \dd x \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} \int \sqrt{k} \, \dd k \\ &= - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{2}{3} k^{3/2}\right) \\ &= - \frac{1}{3} \left(9 - x^2\right)^{3/2}. \\ \end{aligned} \] We have neglected the constant of integration for simplicity; it can simply be added at the end. Therefore, we get \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int x^2 \cdot x \sqrt{9 - x^2} \, \dd x &= -\frac{1}{3}x^2 \left(9 - x^2\right)^{3/2} + \frac{2}{3} \int x \left(9 - x^2\right)^{3/2} \, \dd x. \\ \end{aligned} \] We now have one final task—to integrate \(\int x \left(9 - x^2\right)^{3/2} \, \dd x.\) To do so, we can perform the substitution \(p = 9 - x^2\) (it's getting harder to think of variables to use!) Then, \(\dd p = -2x \, \dd x\) and the integral becomes \[ \begin{aligned} -\frac{1}{2} \int p^{3/2} \, \dd p &= -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{2}{5} p^{5/2}\right) \\ &= -\frac{1}{5} \left(9 - x^2 \right)^{5/2}. \end{aligned} \] Again, we will worry about the constant of integration later. Therefore, plugging in, we obtain—for our final answer— \[ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt] {-\frac{1}{3} x^2 \left(9 - x^2\right)^{3/2} - \frac{2}{15} \left(9 - x^2\right)^{5/2} + C.} \]


EXAMPLE \(2\)

It is known that \(f'(4) = 2\), \(f(0) = 0\), and \(f(4) = 3\). Find the value of \(\displaystyle \int_0^4 x f''(x) \, \dd x.\)

Using integration by parts, we can rewrite the integral by choosing \(u = x\) and \(\dd v = f''(x) \, \dd x.\) Then, \(\dd u = \dd x\) and \(v = f'(x)\) (note that \(1 \, \dd x = \dd x.\)) Using the integration by parts formula, \[ \int u \, \dd v = uv - \int v \, \dd u,\] and plugging in each expression produce \[ x f'(x) \Big|_0^4 - \int_0^4 f'(x) \, \dd x. \] Note that doing integration by parts is not performing a substitution. \(u\) and \(v\) are simply placeholders for our functions of \(x\). Therefore, we do not need to worry about changing the bounds, as we do with substitutions. We then get \[ 4f'(4) - 0 - \left(f(4) - f(0)\right) \] \[ \begin{aligned} &= 4(2) - (3 - 0) \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{5.} \end{aligned} \]



Using integration by parts, we can antidifferentiate functions such as \(\ln(x),\) \(\sin^{-1}(x),\) and \(\tan^{-1}(x)\). For \(\int \ln(x) \, \dd x,\) it appears at first that there is only one part (which is paradoxical because integration by parts requires two parts). However, we can choose \(u = \ln(x)\) and \(\dd v = 1 \, \dd x\). We will then proceed to get \(\dd u = 1/x \, \dd x\) and \(v = x.\) Using the integration by parts formula, \[ \int u \, \dd v = uv - \int v \, \dd u,\] we obtain \[x \ln(x) - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, \dd x\] \[ \begin{aligned} &= x \ln(x) - \int \, \dd x \\ &= x \ln(x) - x + C. \end{aligned} \]


EXAMPLE \(3\)
Evaluate the following integrals.
  1. \(\displaystyle \int \sin^{-1}(x) \, \dd x\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int \cos^{-1}(x) \, \dd x\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \int \tan^{-1}(x) \, \dd x\)
  1. We let \(u = \sin^{-1}(x)\) and \(\dd v = \dd x.\) Then, \(\displaystyle \dd u = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, \dd x\) and \(v = x.\) We then get, from the integration by parts formula, \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int \sin^{-1}(x) \, \dd x &= x \sin^{-1}(x) - \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, \dd x. \\ \end{aligned} \] We can evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\) by performing the substitution \(k = 1 - x^2.\) It then follows that \(\dd k = -2x \, \dd x,\) and the integral becomes \[-\frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} \, \dd k \] \[ \begin{aligned} &= -\frac{1}{2} (2 \sqrt{k}) \\ &= - \sqrt{1 - x^2}. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, we get, for our final answer, \[\bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{x \sin^{-1}(x) + \sqrt{1 - x^2} + C.}\]
  2. We let \(u = \cos^{-1}(x)\) and \(\dd v = \dd x.\) Then, \(\displaystyle \dd u = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, \dd x\) and \(v = x.\) We then have, from the integration by parts formula, \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int \cos^{-1}(x) \, \dd x &= x \cos^{-1}(x) + \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, \dd x. \\ &=\bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{x \cos^{-1}(x) - \sqrt{1 - x^2} + C.} \end{aligned} \]
  3. Let \(u = \tan^{-1}(x)\) and \(v = \dd x.\) It follows that \(\displaystyle \dd u = \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \, \dd x\) and \(v = x.\) Then, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \int u \, \dd v &= uv - \int v \, \dd u \\ \int \tan^{-1}(x) \, \dd x &= x \tan^{-1}(x) - \int \frac{x}{1 + x^2} \, \dd x. \\ \end{aligned} \] To evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \frac{x}{1 + x^2} \, \dd x,\) we can let \(k = 1 + x^2.\) Then, \(\dd k = 2x \, \dd x,\) and the integral becomes \[\frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{k} \, \dd k\] \[ \begin{aligned} &= \frac{1}{2} \ln|k| + C \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \ln(1 + x^2) + C. \end{aligned} \] We therefore get, for our final answer, \[ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{ x \tan^{-1}(x) - \frac{1}{2} \ln(1 + x^2) + C. } \] (The absolute value bars were extraneous because \(1 + x^2\) is always positive.)


There is a simpler way of performing integration by parts, known as the tabular method. This method is particularly useful for situations that warrant performing integration by parts multiple times. To utilize it, we draw a two-column table—with the left column (labeled "D") representing the quantities we differentiate and the right column (labeled "I") representing the quantities we integrate. Consider the integral \(\int x^2 e^x \, \dd x,\) which would warrant performing integration by parts twice. Instead, we will utilize tabular integration. We will pick the quantity to differentiate to be \(x^2\) and the quantity to integrate to be \(e^x,\) which correspond to \(u\) and \(\dd v,\) respectively.


\(D\) \(I\)
\(x^2\) \(e^x\)

We now add more rows. In the second row, after differentiating \(x^2\) and integrating \(e^x,\) we obtain \(2x\) and \(e^x\) for the \(D\) and \(I\) columns, respectively. Then, for the third row—after repeating this procedure—we acquire \(2\) and \(e^x.\) Similarly, for the fourth row, we get \(0\) and \(e^x.\) We stop if we have \(0\) in the "\(D\)" column. Now, we assign signs to each row of the table, starting with positive and alternating on the way down. We then pair expressions by drawing arrows, as shown by Fig. 5.1-1.
