10.2 — Infinite Geometric Series

We have seen finite geometric series and their applications, so now let us step up and discuss infinite geometric series. An infinite geometric series is in the form $$ \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} ar^n = ar^k + ar^{k+1} + ar^{k+2} + \cdots , k = 0, 1, 2, 3, \dots, $$ where \(r\) is the common ratio (the multiple by which each term varies from each other). The defining feature of a geometric series is that, for any sufficiently large \(n\), \(\displaystyle r = \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\). This definition asserts that the terms in a geometric series vary by a multiple, \(r\), in this case. It seems paradoxical at first to have convergence in a sum of numbers that change exponentially, but upon further inspection it is clear that convergence can only occur if the terms are decreasing; i.e., \(|r| \lt 1\). If \(|r| \lt 1\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} r^n = 0 \). This result agrees with the divergence test, which states that a series \(\sum a_n\) diverges if \(\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \ne 0\).

We know that the value to which a finite geometric series, \(\displaystyle \sum_{i = 0}^n ar^i\), converges is given by $$ \frac{a \left(1 - r^n\right)}{1 - r}. $$ In this case, for an infinite geometric series, we will consider the limit as \(n\) approaches infinity. We also let \(|r|\) be less than \(1\) to establish convergence for the series. Taking the limit and considering \(|r| \lt 1 \), we obtain $$ \begin{aligned} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty a r^n &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a \left(1 - r^n\right)}{1 - r} \\ &= \frac{a}{1 - r}. \end{aligned} $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$ To generalize this result for any starting index, we notice that \(a\) is the first term when dealing with the starting index \(n = 0\). We can account for any disparity in the starting index by writing the general form $$ \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r}. $$


FORMULA FOR AN INFINITE GEOMETRIC SERIES

$$ \sum_{n = k}^\infty a r^n = \frac{ar^k}{1 - r} = \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r}. $$

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 1

Determine whether each of the following series converges or diverges. If a series converges, evaluate the sum.

  1. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{5} \right)^n \)
  2. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{8^n} \)
  3. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} 4^n \cdot e^{-n} \)
  4. \(\displaystyle 2 - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{9} - \frac{2}{27} + \cdots\)
  5. \(\displaystyle \frac{5}{16} + \frac{5}{64} + \frac{5}{256} + \frac{5}{1024} + \cdots \)
  6. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{2^{n - 2}}{3^n} \)

  1. We can use the given formula for a geometric series to evaluate this sum, as we notice that the common ratio, \(r\), is \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{5}\) in this case, less than \(1\). Therefore, we know that the sum is convergent, and to find the value to which it converges, we plug in to get $$ \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1-r} = \frac{1(\frac{1}{5})^0}{1-\frac{1}{5}} = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{5}{4}}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  2. We can rewrite the sum as \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^\infty 2 \left(\displaystyle \frac{1}{8}\right)^n\). We notice that the common ratio, \(r\), is \(\frac{1}{8}\), which is less between \(-1\) and \(1\). Therefore, the series converges to the value $$ \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r} $$ $$ = \frac{2 \left(\frac{1}{8}\right)}{1 - \frac{1}{8}} = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{2}{7}}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  3. This series can be rewritten as \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \left(\frac{4}{e}\right)^n\). We notice immediately that the common ratio, \(r\), is \(\displaystyle \frac{4}{e}\) in this case. Because \(|r| \gt 1\), we conclude that the series diverges. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  4. To find the common ratio \(r\), we can utilize the defining property of a geometric series, \(\displaystyle r = \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\). In this case, we see that \(r\) can be found by evaluating \(\displaystyle \frac{-2/3}{2}\), which is \(\displaystyle -\frac{1}{3}\). We can now rewrite the terms using summation notation as $$ \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} 2 \Big(-\frac{1}{3}\Big)^n, $$ which has a common ratio of \(\displaystyle r = -\frac{1}{3}\), whose absolute value is less than \(1\). Therefore, we plug into the formula to obtain $$ \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1-r} = \frac{2(-\frac{1}{3})^0}{1-(-\frac{1}{3})} = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{3}{2}}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  5. We can either rewrite the sum using summation notation as \(\displaystyle 5\sum_{n = 2}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{4} \right)^n \), or we can rearrange the series as $$ \frac{5}{16} \left(1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{64} + \cdots \right), $$ which can be written using summation notation as \(\displaystyle \frac{5}{16} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{4^n} \). Either way, we will obtain $$ \frac{5/16}{1 - \frac{1}{4}} = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{5}{12}}.$$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  6. Using exponent properties, we can rewrite the sum as $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{2^n \cdot 2^{-2}}{3^n} $$ $$ = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n $$ $$ = \frac{1/4}{1 - \frac{2}{3}} = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{3}{4}}.$$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

We should be a cautious for disguised geometric series. Beware of any series that do not start at index \(n = 0\)—in these cases, it is advisable to factor out a coefficient to obtain a starting term of \(1\), as we did in part V of example \(1\). To test whether a series \(\displaystyle \sum a_n\) is geometric, utilize the property that $$ \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n} = r. $$ It is also important to use the exponent properties $$ \left(\frac{1}{b}\right)^n = \frac{1}{b^n} \, \, \, \, \textrm{and} \, \, \, \, a^n \cdot b^{n} = \left(ab\right)^n, $$ as shown by parts II. and IV. of example 1, respectively. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 2

Find the values of \(k\) for which the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left(\frac{k^2 + 1}{5}\right)^n\) converges. Derive an expression for the value of the convergent series in terms of \(k\).

We know that the series will converge if \(|r| \lt 1 \). Setting up this inequality shows that $$ \frac{k^2 + 1}{5} \lt 1 \implies k^2 \lt 4. $$ $$ |k| \lt 2 \implies -2 \lt k \lt 2. $$ Additionally, the series converges to the value $$ \frac{(\frac{k^2+1}{5})^2}{1-\frac{k^2+1}{5}} = \frac{(k^2+1)^2}{25-5(k^2+1)} \, \, \mathrm{for} \, -2 \lt k \lt 2. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$


Now let us discuss how to express repeating decimals in terms of fractions. Consider, for example, the repeating decimal \(\displaystyle 0.5\overline{3}\). We can rewrite the decimal as the series $$ 0.5 + 0.03 + 0.003 + 0.0003 + \cdots, $$ which we can rewrite using summation notation as $$ 0.5 + 0.03\sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \left(0.1\right)^n. $$ The common ratio, \(r\), in this case, is \(0.1\). Therefore, the series converges to the value $$ 0.5 + \frac{0.03}{1 - 0.1} $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{3/100}{9/10} $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{30} $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{8}{15}}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
Example 3

Express the following repeating decimals as fractions.

  1. \(0.7\overline{6}\)
  2. \(0.64\overline{1}\)
  3. \(0.1\overline{2}\)

  1. $$ \begin{aligned} 0.7\overline{6} &= 0.7 + 0.06 + 0.006 + 0.0006 + \cdots \\ &= 0.7 + 0.06\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.7 + \frac{0.06}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.7 + \frac{6/100}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{7}{10} + \frac{1}{15} \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{23}{30}}.\\ \end{aligned} $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  2. $$ \begin{aligned} 0.64\bar{1} &= 0.64 + 0.001 + 0.0001 + 0.00001 + \cdots \\ &= 0.64 + 0.001\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.64 + \frac{0.001}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.64 + \frac{1/1000}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{64}{100} + \frac{1}{900} \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{577}{900}}.\\ \end{aligned} $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  3. $$ \begin{aligned} 0.1\overline{2} &= 0.1 + 0.02 + 0.002 + 0.0002 + \cdots \\ &= 0.1 + 0.02\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.1 + \frac{0.02}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.1 + \frac{2/100}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{45} \\ &= \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{11}{90}}.\\ \end{aligned} $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$


SECTION SUMMARY

  • An infinite geometric series is in the form \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n = ar^k + ar^{k + 1} + ar^{k + 2} + \cdots\).
  • An infinite geometric series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n\) converges if \(|r| \lt 1\), and its sum is given by the formula \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r}\).
  • For disguised geometric series, utilize the properties that \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{b^n} = \left(\frac{1}{b}\right)^n\), where \(b\) is non-zero, and \(\displaystyle a^n b^n = \left(ab\right)^n\).
  • The common ratio is given by \(\displaystyle \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\).
  • Geometric series can be used to write repeating decimals as fractions.
