1.1 — Defining a Limit

Let's now begin our rigorous study of calculus! In this section we will discuss the limit, one of the three fundamental tools of calculus. The notation of a limit is given by

$$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L, $$

where \(L\) is the value to which \(f(x)\) converges as \(x\) approaches (is made arbitrarily close to) \(a\). This notation is read as: "The limit, as \(x\) approaches \(2\), of \(f(x)\) equals \(L\)." A limit can also be expressed with arrow notation: $$ \textrm{as} \, \, x \to a, \, f(x) \to L. $$ In other words, as \(x\) gets closer and closer to \(a\), \(f(x)\) gets closer and closer to \(L\). For the moment this is an informal but working definition, and in later sections we will present a formal definition of a limit.


DEFINITION OF LIMIT (INFORMAL)

If a continuous function \(f(x)\) can be made infinitely close to a finite value \(L\) as \(x\) is made sufficiently close to \(a\) but not equal to \(a\), then $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L. $$ This notation is read as: "The limit, as \(x\) approaches \(a\), of \(f(x)\) equals \(L\)." Using arrow notation, a limit can also be expressed by saying: $$ \textrm{as} \, \, x \to a, \, f(x) \to L. $$


As an example, consider the table in Fig. 1-1.1., which shows values of \(f(x)\) for certain values of \(x\) near \(1\). Let's analyze the data below and use them to approximate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1} f(x)\).


\(x\) \(0.9\) \(0.99\) \(0.999\) \(0.9999\) \(1.00001\) \(1.0001\) \(1.001\) \(1.01\)
\(f(x)\) \(3.9\) \(3.99\) \(3.999\) \(3.9999\) \(4.00001\) \(4.0001\) \(4.001\) \(4.01\)

Fig. 1-1.1. A table shows values of \(f(x)\) for certain values of \(x\) near \(x = 1\).


From the table, we observe that \(f(x)\) approaches \(4\) as \(x\) approaches \(1\). We can therefore estimate that $$ \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 4 $$ since \(f(x)\) gets closer to closer to \(4\) as \(x\) gets closer and closer to \(1\). Note, however, that \(f(x)\) does not need to be defined at \(x = 1\) for the limit to be \(4\). \(f(x)\) may have a hole at \(x = 4\), but the limit simply describes the behavior of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(1\).

Fig. 1-1.2. The function \(f\) contains a hole at the point \((a,L)\).

To illustrate this point, consider the graph in Fig. 1-1.2., which has a hole at the point \((a, L)\). Nevertheless, \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\), because the function approaches \(L\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\). Limits simply model a function's behavior, so it is irrelevant that \(f(a)\) is undefined—\(f(x)\) can still be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) for \(x\)-values sufficiently close to \(a\).


Example 1

The following table shows selected values of \(g\). Use these data to approximate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 3} g(x)\).

\(x\) \(2.9\) \(2.99\) \(2.999\) \(2.9999\) \(3.00001\) \(3.0001\) \(3.001\) \(3.01\)
\(g(x)\) \(3.49\) \(3.499\) \(3.4999\) \(3.49999\) \(3.50001\) \(3.5001\) \(3.501\) \(3.51\)

It is clear that \(g\) approaches \(3.5\) as \(x\) approaches \(3\). As \(x\) gets closer and closer to \(3\), \(g\) gets closer and closer to \(3.5\). Thus, $$ \lim_{x \to 3} g(x) \approx 3.5 .$$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$


Fig. 1-1.3. The graph of the function \(y = x^2\) is shown.

Consider Fig. 1-1.3., in which the graph of \(y = x^2\) is shown. It is clear that, as \(x\) approaches \(2\), \(x^2\) will approach \(4\) (it is a point on the graph, after all!) We therefore conclude that if a function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\), then \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\). This procedure of evaluating limits is known as direct substitution. In this case, $$ \lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = (2)^2 = 4. $$


DIRECT SUBSTITUTION

If \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\).


Example 2

Evaluate each of the following limits.

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 5} (x^3 - 4x^2 + 9x - 16) \)
  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to e} (2 + \ln(x)) \)
  3. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \left(4 - \sin(3x)\right) \)

  1. The function \(x^3 - 4x^2 + 9x - 16\) is a polynomial and thus continuous for all \(x\). Since it is continuous at \(x = 5\), we can perform a direct substitution by plugging \(x = 5\) into the function to evaluate the limit: $$ \lim_{x \to 5} (x^3 - 4x^2 + 9x - 16) = ((5)^3 - 4(5)^2 + 9(5) -16) = \bbox[border: 2pt solid white, 2px]{54}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  2. The function \(2 + \ln(x)\) is continuous for all \(x > 0\), so we can perform a direct substitution by plugging \(x = e\) into the function: $$ \lim_{x \to e} (2 + \ln(x)) = 2 + \ln(e) = \bbox[border: 2pt solid white, 2px]{3}. $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
  3. \(4 - \sin(3x)\) is continuous for all \(x\). Thus, we can perform direct substitution by plugging in \(x = \pi/2\) to obtain $$ \begin{aligned} \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \left(4 - \sin(3x)\right) &= 4 - \sin{\left(\frac{3 \pi}{2}\right)}\\ &= 4 - (-1) \\ &= \bbox[border: 2pt solid white, 2px]{5}. \\ \end{aligned} $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$ $$


Let's now introduce one-sided limits. A left-hand-side (LHS) limit is expressed as $$ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x), $$ and a right-hand-side (RHS) limit is expressed as $$ \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x). $$ These one-sided limits represent the value to which \(f(x)\) converges as \(x\) approaches \(a\) from a certain side (left or right). In order for a limit to exist, the LHS and RHS limits must be equivalent; i.e., \(\lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x)\) must equal \(\lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x)\) in order for \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) to exist. When given a limit, we should first consider the one-sided limits in order to establish that they are equivalent, indicating that the limit does exist.

Fig. 1-1.4. The function \(f\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x = 4\).

As an example, consider the function \(f\), whose graph is shown in Fig.1-1.4. \(f(x)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x = 4\). As \(x\) approaches \(4\) from the left side (\(x \lt 4\) ), \(f(x)\) approaches \(6\). Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to 4^{-}} f(x) = 6\). We also observe that, as \(x\) approaches \(4\) from the right side (\(x \gt 4\)), \(f(x)\) approaches \(5\). Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to 4^{+}} f(x) = 5\). Because \(\lim_{x \to 4^{-}} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to 4^{+}} f(x), \) we state that \(\lim_{x \to 4} f(x)\) does not exist.


ONE-SIDED LIMITS

  • The value that \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x\) approaches \(a\) from the left side (\(x \lt a\)) is notated by \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)\).
  • The value that \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x\) approaches \(a\) from the right side (\(x \gt a\)) is notated by \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\).
  • If \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \ne \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \) does not exist.


Example 3

Fig. 1-1.5.

In Fig. 1-1.5., a function \(f\) is shown. Find \(\lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x)\). Then, find \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\) or state that it does not exist.

To find \(\lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x)\), we analyze the behavior of \(f\) as \(x\) approaches \(5\) from the left side (i.e., \(x \lt 5\)) We notice that, as \(x\) approaches \(5\) from the left, \(f(x)\) approaches \(7\). Therefore, $$ \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = 7. $$ Similarly, as \(x\) approaches \(5\) from the right (i.e., \(x \gt 5\)), we notice that \(f(x)\) approaches \(8\). Therefore, $$ \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = 8. $$ Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) \ne \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x), \lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\) does not exist. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$


Fig. 1-1.6. The function \(f(x) = 1/x\) is shown.

There are three scenarios in which limits fail to exist. Consider the limit \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\). The first scenario is when \(\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \ne \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\), as previously established. The second and third scenarios feature functions that are either unbounded or oscillate. In either case, the function will never settle to a finite value. Consider, as an example, the function \(f(x) = 1/x^2\), whose graph is shown in Fig. 1-1.6. From the graph, we observe that \(f(x)\) contains a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0\) and approaches infinity on either side of the asymptote. We therefore note that $$ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^2} \to \infty. $$ We therefore say that \(\lim_{x \to 0} 1/x^2\) does not exist, as the limit is unbounded.

Fig. 1-1.7. The function \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) is shown.

Additionally, consider the function \(f(x) = \sin(x)\), shown in Fig. 1-1.7. We understand that \(f(x)\) continuously oscillates and will never settle to a final value as \(x\) approaches infinity. Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \sin(x)\) does not exist.


WHEN A LIMIT DOES NOT EXIST

  1. A limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist if \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \ne \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\).
  2. If \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \to \pm \infty\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist.
  3. If \(f\) oscillates and does not tend to a final value, then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \pm \infty} f(x)\) does not exist.


Example 4

For each of the following, evaluate the limit or state that it does not exist.

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2}{x} \)
  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \cos(2x - 1) \)
  3. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \left(x^2 - 4x + 9\right) \)

  1. As \(x\) grows to infinity, \(2/x\) will tend to \(0\) (\(y = 0\) is the vertical asymptote of the function.) Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} 2/x\) does not exist.
  2. The function will continuously oscillate and, as \(x\) approaches infinity, will never settle to a final value. Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \cos(2x - 1)\) does not exist.
  3. The end behavior of the function \(y = x^2 - 4x + 9\) is that, as \(x \to \infty, y \to \infty\). Therefore, the limit tends to infinity and does not exist.


SECTION SUMMARY

  • A limit gives the value that a function \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x\) is made arbitrarily close to a value \(a\) but not equal to \(a\).
  • If a function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \) (direct substitution).
  • To determine whether a limit exists, first consider the one-sided limits. If the left-hand-side (LHS) and right-hand-side (RHS) limits are not equivalent, then the limit does not exist; i.e., if \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \ne \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\), then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist.
  • If a limit tends to \(\pm \infty\), then it does not exist.
  • If a function \(f(x)\) is oscillating and never tends to a fixed value, then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \pm \infty} f(x)\) does not exist.
