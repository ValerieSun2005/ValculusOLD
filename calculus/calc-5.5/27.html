5.5 Exercises — Problem 27

\((27)\) For a continuous function \(g(x)\), it is known that \(g(0) = 0\) and \(g(4) = 12\). Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_0^4 \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \, \textrm{d} x \) or show that it diverges.

We immediately notice that the integral can be evaluated using the substitution \(u = g(x)\). Then, \(\displaystyle{d} u = g'(x) \, \textrm{d} x \), and we obtain $$ \int_{x = 0}^{x = 4} \frac{1}{u} \, \textrm{d} u. $$ We will now change the bounds of the integral. For the lower bound, when \(x = 0\), \(u = g(0) = 0\). For the upper bound, when \(x = 4\), \(u = g(4) = 12\). Therefore, we get $$ \int_0^{12} \frac{1}{u} \, \textrm{d} u. $$ Notice that this is a Type II improper integral because \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{u}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(u = 0\). Therefore, we consider $$ \lim_{k \to 0^+} \int_k^{12} \frac{1}{u} \, \textrm{d} u. $$ Antidifferentiating and plugging in the bounds produce $$ \lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln(u) \Big]_k^{12} $$ $$ = \ln(12) - \lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln(k). $$ We notice that \(\displaystyle - \lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln(k)\) approaches \(-\infty\) and thus does not exist. Because the limit does not exist, $$ \int_0^4 \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$
