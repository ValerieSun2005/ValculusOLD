5.5 Exercises — Problem 4

\((4)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_2^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x \) or show that it diverges.

Be careful—this integral is improper! There is a vertical asymptote at \(x = 4\), so we must break the integral as $$ \lim_{k \to 4^-} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x + \lim_{k \to 4^+} \int_k^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x. $$ If either of these limits does not exist, then \(\displaystyle \int_2^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x \) diverges.

Let us first consider \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to 4^-} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x\), which is a Type II improper integral. Antidifferentiating yields $$ \lim_{k \to 4^-} \ln |x - 4| \Big]_{2}^k $$ $$ \underbrace{\lim_{k \to 4^-} \ln|k - 4|}_{-\infty} - \ln(2) $$

Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to 4^-} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x\) does not exist, $$ \int_2^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$ Note that we do not need to consider the second limit, \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to 4^+} \int_k^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \, \textrm{d} x\), because we already established that at least one of the limits does not exist.
