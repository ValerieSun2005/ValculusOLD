5.5 Exercises — Problem 1

\((1)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \, \textrm{d} x \) or show that it diverges.

Notice that this integral is a Type I improper integral—with an upper bound of infinity. Therefore, we will insert a variable, \(k\), substitute it for the upper bound, and take the limit as \(k\) approaches infinity. We then get $$ \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \, \textrm{d} x = \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x + 9} \, \textrm{d} x $$ Notice that an antiderivative of \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{x + 9} \) is \(\displaystyle \ln(x + 9)\) (we can neglect the absolute value bars because the integral is over positive values.) Thus, we obtain $$ \lim_{k \to \infty} \ln(x + 9) \Big]_2^k $$ $$ = \lim_{k \to \infty} \ln(k + 9) - \ln(2 + 9) $$ $$ = \underbrace{\lim_{k \to \infty} \ln(k + 9)}_{\infty} - \ln 11. $$ Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to \infty} \ln(k + 9)\) does not exist, $$ \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$
