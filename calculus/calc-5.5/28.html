5.5 Exercises — Problem 28

\((28)\) It is known that \(\displaystyle g(x) \leq q(x)\), where \(g\) and \(q\) are continuous for all \(x\). It is known that \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty q(x) \, \textrm{d}x \) is finite. Explain why \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty g(x) \, \textrm{d}x \) could be divergent.

At first, it may be obvious to assume that \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty g(x) \, \textrm{d}x \) converges too, as it is bounded by a positive, finite value. However, this answer fails to consider the possibility of \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty g(x) \, \textrm{d}x \) diverging to \(-\infty\).

The comparison test to determine covergence is not applicable here because it would, for this problem, require the form $$ 0 \lt g(x) \leq q(x). $$ However, it is unknown if \(g\) is positive, so \(\displaystyle \int_2^\infty g(x) \, \textrm{d}x \) could diverge to \(-\infty\).
