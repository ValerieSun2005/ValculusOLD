5.5 Exercises — Problem 32

\((32)\) A particle continuously moves along the \(x\)-axis at a velocity given by \(\displaystyle v(t) = \frac{44}{t^{2/3}}\) for \(t \gt 1\), where \(v(t)\) is measured in meters per second and \(t\) is measured in seconds. Show that the particle will travel an infinite distance.

For this problem, it is necessary to consider the displacement of the particle, which is given by $$ \int_1^\infty v(t) \, \textrm{d} t. $$ If this improper integral converges, then the particle will cover a finite distance; if it diverges, then the particle will travel an infinite distance. $$ \int_1^\infty \frac{44}{t^{2/3}} \, \textrm{d} t $$ $$ = 44 \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_1^k t^{-2/3} \, \textrm{d} t $$ $$ = 44 \cdot 3 \lim_{k \to \infty} t^{1/3} \Big]_1^k $$ $$ = 132 \underbrace{\lim_{k \to \infty} \sqrt[3]{k}}_{\infty} - 132. $$ Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to \infty} \sqrt[3]{k}\) does not exist, \(\displaystyle \int_1^\infty v(t) \, \textrm{d} t\) diverges, and the particle will travel an infinite distance.
