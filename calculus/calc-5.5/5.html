5.5 Exercises — Problem 5

\((5)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^{\pi/2} \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) or show that it diverges.

Let us rewrite the integral as $$ \lim_{k \to -\infty} \int_k^{\pi/2} \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Antidifferentiating and plugging in the bounds give $$ \lim_{k \to -\infty} \sin(x) \Big]_k^{\pi/2} $$ $$ = \sin \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) - \lim_{k \to -\infty} \sin(k). $$ \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to -\infty} \sin(k)\) does not exist, as \(\sin()\) will continuously oscillate and will never converge to a certain value. Therefore, because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to -\infty} \sin(k)\) does not exist, $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\pi/2} \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$
