5.5 Exercises — Problem 8

\((8)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sec(\theta) \tan(\theta) \, \textrm{d} \theta \) or show that it diverges.

Notice that the integrand has a vertical asymptote at \(\displaystyle \theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\). Therefore, we will consider $$ \lim_{k \to \pi/2^-} \int_0^{k} \sec(\theta) \tan(\theta) \, \textrm{d} \theta. $$ Recall that \(\displaystyle \int \sec(\theta) \tan(\theta) \, \textrm{d} \theta = \sec(\theta) + C\). Therefore, antidifferentiating, we get $$ \lim_{k \to \pi/2^-} \sec(\theta) \Big]_0^k $$ $$ = \lim_{k \to \pi/2^-} \sec(k) - \sec(0) $$ $$ = \underbrace{\lim_{k \to \pi/2^-} \frac{1}{\cos(k)}}_{\infty} - 1. $$ Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to \pi/2^-} \sec(k)\) does not exist, $$ \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sec(\theta) \tan(\theta) \, \textrm{d} \theta \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$
