5.5 Exercises — Problem 7

\((7)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_{-2}^4 \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \, \textrm{d} x \) or show that it diverges.

Proceed with caution—the integrand contains a vertical asymptote at \(x = 1\). Thus, we will break the integral into $$ \lim_{k \to 1^-} \int_{-2}^k \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \, \textrm{d} x + \lim_{k \to 1^+} \int_k^4 \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Let us consider \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to 1^-} \int_{-2}^k \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \, \textrm{d} x\). Antidifferentiating yields $$ \lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{(x - 1)^3} \Big]_{-2}^k $$ $$ = \underbrace{\lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{(k - 1)^3}}_{\infty} - \frac{-1}{(-2 - 1)^3}. $$ Because \(\displaystyle \lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{(k - 1)^3}\) does not exist, $$ \int_{-2}^4 \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \, \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\textrm{diverges}.} $$
