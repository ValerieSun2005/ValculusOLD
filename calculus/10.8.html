10.8 — Ratio and Root Tests

In this brief section, we will discuss the ratio test. Consider the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\). To determine its convergence, we consider $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\right|, $$ whose value we will call "\(L\)".

The ratio test states that if \(L \lt 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely; if \(L \gt 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) diverges; and if \(L = 1\), then the test is indeterminate.


RATIO TEST

Let \(\displaystyle L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n} \right| \).

  1. If \(L \lt 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) is absolutely convergent.
  2. If \(L \gt 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) diverges.
  3. If \(L = 1\), then the test is indeterminate.

Many of the example problems that we will do involve factorials. In those cases, it is important to remember the property that, for sufficiently large \(n\), $$ n! = n (n - 1)! .$$


Example 1

Use the ratio test to determine whether each of the following series converge or diverge.

  1. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{e^n}{n!} \)
  2. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{3^{2n}}{8^n + 3} \)
  3. \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n }{n^2 + 2} \)

  1. We will consider \(\displaystyle L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\right|\), where \(\displaystyle a_n = \frac{e^n}{n!} \) in this case. Therefore, $$ \begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n} \right| &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{e^{n + 1}}{(n + 1)!} \div \frac{e^n}{n!} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{e^n \cdot e}{(n + 1) n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{e^n} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{e}{n + 1} \right| \\ &= e \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{1}{n + 1} \right| \\ &= 0. \end{align} $$ Because \(L \lt 1 \), we conclude that the series is absolutely convergent (and therefore converges).
  2. In this case, we let \(\displaystyle a_n = \frac{3^{2n}}{8^n + 3}\). Thus, $$ \begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\right| &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{3^{2(n + 1)}}{8^{n + 1} + 3} \div \frac{3^{2n}}{8^n + 3} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{3^{2n + 2}}{8^{n + 1} + 3} \cdot \frac{8^n + 3}{3^{2n}} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{9^n \cdot 9}{8^n \cdot 8 + 3} \cdot \frac{8^n + 3}{9^n} \right| \\ &= 9 \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{8^n + 3}{8^n \cdot 8 + 3} \cdot \right| \\ &= \frac{9}{8} \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{8^n + 3}{8^n + 3/8}\right| \\ &= \frac{9}{8}. \end{align} $$ Because \(L \gt 1 \), we conclude that the series diverges.
  3. We notice that the term \(\displaystyle (-1)^n\) is an alternator—it simply changes the sign of each term. When we consider \(\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n} \right|\), where \(\displaystyle a_n = \frac{(-1)^n }{n^2 + 2}\) in this case, we can simply neglect it (it will be in absolute value bars and therefore always be positive.) We then get $$ \begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\right| &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{1}{(n + 1)^2 + 2} \div \frac{1}{n^2 + 2} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{1}{n^2 + 2n + 1} \cdot \frac{n^2 + 2}{1} \right| \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{n^2 + 2}{n^2 + 2n + 1} \right| \\ &= 1. \end{align} $$ Because \(L = 1\), the test is inconclusive. We cannot use the ratio test to determine whether or not this series converges.
  4. \(\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n^2 - 4}{7n^2 + 1} = \frac{3}{7}\), so \(\{a_n\}\) converges to \(\displaystyle \frac{3}{7}\).


Example 2

Determine the values of \(k\) for which \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 2}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{k^{3n}}{e^{n + 4}} \) converges.

SECTION SUMMARY

  • An infinite geometric series is in the form \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n = ar^k + ar^{k + 1} + ar^{k + 2} + \cdots\).
  • An infinite geometric series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n\) converges if \(|r| \lt 1\), and its sum is given by the formula \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r}\).
  • For disguised geometric series, utilize the properties that \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{b^n} = \left(\frac{1}{b}\right)^n\), where \(b\) is non-zero, and \(\displaystyle a^n b^n = \left(ab\right)^n\).
  • The common ratio is given by \(\displaystyle \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}\).
  • Geometric series can be used to write repeating decimals as fractions.
