5.2 Exercises — Problem 12

\((12)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \sin^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x \).

Using the power-reduction formula for sine, $$ \sin^2(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 - \cos(2x)\right], $$ we obtain $$ \int \left(\frac{1}{2}\left[1 - \cos(2x)\right]\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \frac{1}{4} \int \left(1 - 2 \cos(2x) + \cos^2(2x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ At this point we will need to use the power-reduction formula for cosine to further reduce the integral: $$ \cos^2(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left[1 + \cos(2x)\right] $$ $$ \therefore \cos^2(2x) = \frac{1}{2} \left[1 + \cos(4x)\right]. $$ Substituting gives $$ \frac{1}{4} \int \left(1 - 2 \cos(2x) + \frac{1}{2} \left[1 + \cos(4x)\right] \right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \frac{1}{4} \int \left(\frac{3}{2} - 2 \cos(2x) + \frac{1}{2} \cos(4x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \frac{1}{4} \left(\frac{3}{2} x - \sin(2x) + \frac{1}{8} \sin(4x) \right) + C $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{3}{8} x - \frac{1}{4} \sin(2x) + \frac{1}{32} \sin(4x) + C.} $$
