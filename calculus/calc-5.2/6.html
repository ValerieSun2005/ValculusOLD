5.2 Exercises — Problem 6

\((6)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \sec^3(x) \tan^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x \).

The integral can be rewritten, using the Pythagorean identity, as $$ \int \sec(x) \sec^2(x) \tan(x) \tan^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec(x) \tan(x) \sec^2(x) (\sec^2(x) - 1) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Recall that $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} \left[\sec(x)\right] = \sec(x) \tan(x). $$ Therefore, the substitution \(u = \sec(x)\) will help clear the \(\sec(x) \tan(x)\) term.

Let \(u = \sec(x)\).

$$ \textrm{d} u = \sec(x) \tan(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We then get $$ \int u^2(u^2 - 1) \, \textrm{d} u. $$ $$ = \int \left(u^4 - u^2 \right) \, \textrm{d} u. $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} - \frac{u^3}{3} + C. $$ Substituting back, we obtain $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{1}{5} \sec^5(x) - \frac{1}{3}\sec^3(x) + C.} $$
