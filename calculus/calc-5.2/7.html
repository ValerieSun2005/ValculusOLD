5.2 Exercises — Problem 7

\((7)\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \sin^5(x) \sec^6(x) \, \textrm{d} x \).

We should rewrite the integral in terms of \(\sin()\) and \(\cos()\) to simplify it. $$ \int \sin^5(x) \sec^6(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sin^5(x) \frac{1}{\cos^6(x)} \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We see that the integral is equivalent to $$ \int \sec(x) \tan^5(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which we can rewrite, using the Pythagorean identity, as $$ \int \sec(x) \tan(x) \left(\sec^2(x) - 1\right)^2. $$ Now, let \(u = \sec(x)\). Then, \(\textrm{d} u = \sec(x) \tan(x) \, \textrm{d} x\). The integral then becomes $$ \int \left(u^2 - 1\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \int \left(u^4 - 2u^2 + 1 \right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} - \frac{2u^3}{3} + u + C. $$ Substituting back gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{1}{5} \sec^5(x) - \frac{2}{3}\sec^3(x) + \sec(x) + C.} $$
