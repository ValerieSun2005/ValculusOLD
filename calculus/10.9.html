10.8 — Integral Test

In this section we will discuss the Integral Test, which can be thought of as a last-resort convergence test for when other methods are inconclusive. We will first be introduced with the test and then prove it at the end of the section. The integral test is used to show that the harmonic series, which we introduced all the way back in the start of this chapter, diverges.

INTEGRAL TEST:

Let \(f(x)\) be a continuous, positive, and monotonically decreasing function on the interval \([k, \infty)\) such that \(f(n) = a_n\).

$$ \textrm{If} \int_{k}^{\infty} f(x) \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \textrm{diverges, then so does} \sum_{n = k}^{\infty} a_n. $$ $$ \textrm{If} \int_{k}^{\infty} f(x) \, \textrm{d} x \, \, \textrm{converges, then so does} \sum_{n = k}^{\infty} a_n. $$

Example 1

Determine whether the following series converges or diverges: $$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n^{2}e^{-n^3}. $$

For this problem notice that the function \(x^{2}e^{-x^3}\) is continuous, positive for all \(x\), and monotonically decreasing on the interval \([2, \infty)\). If we were not sure if the function was decreasing, we could have taken its derivative to verify that it is always negative on our interval: $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}\left(x^{2}e^{-x^3}\right) = 2xe^{-x^3} - 3x^{4} e^{-x^3} $$ $$ = xe^{-x^3}\underbrace{(2-3x^3).}_{< 0 \, \textrm{for all} \, x \geq 2 } $$ We can therefore use the integral test for this problem: $$ \int_{2}^{\infty} x^2 e^{-x^3} \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Remember—if this improper integral converges, then the series also converges; if the integral diverges, then the series also diverges. $$ = \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \int_{2}^{\beta} x^2 e^{-x^3} \, \textrm{d} x. $$ $$ \textrm{Let} \, u = x^3 \implies \textrm{d} u = 3x^2 \, \textrm{d} x \implies \textrm{d} x = \frac{\textrm{d} u}{3x^2}. $$ $$ = \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \int_{8}^{\beta} e^{-u} \, \textrm{d}u. $$ $$ = -\lim_{\beta \to 8} \frac{1}{e^u} \Big]_{8}^{\beta} = -\lim_{\beta \to \infty}\Big(\frac{1}{e^{\beta}} - \frac{1}{8}\Big) = \frac{1}{8}. $$

The improper integral converges, so by the integral test the series also converges.

Example 2

Determine whether the following series converges or diverges: $$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n\ln{(n)}}. $$

For this problem let \(\displaystyle f(x) = \frac{1}{x\ln{(x)}} \). Notice that \(f(x)\) is continuous and positive for \(x \in [2, \infty)\). To determine whether \(f(x)\) is decreasing on the interval, we see that $$ f'(x) = \frac{-1-\ln{(x)}}{(x\ln{(x)})^2} < 0 \, \, \mathrm{for} \, x \in [e, \infty). $$

Now let's compare the the series to the improper integral of \(f\): $$ \int_{2}^{\infty} f(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \int_{2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x \ln(x)} \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which we may evaluate by performing a substitution with \(\displaystyle u = \ln(x)\). $$ \int_{2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x \ln(x)} \, \textrm{d} x = \int_{\ln(2)}^{\infty} \frac{1}{u} \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \ln(u) \Big]_{\ln(2)}^{\infty} \implies \textrm{diverges.} $$

Because \(\displaystyle \int_{2}^{\infty} f(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) diverges, so does \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln(n)}\) by the integral test.

Example 3

Let \(\displaystyle a_n = \frac{1}{n^p}\). Verify that if \(p \leq 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum a_n\) diverges and that if \(p > 1\), then \(\displaystyle \sum a_n\) converges.

We'll now be showing why the harmonic series diverges! Let's consider the case where \(p > 1\): \(a_n\) will be positive and monotonically decreasing for all \(n \geq 1\). Now we consider the improper integral $$ \int_{a}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, \textrm{d} x, $$ where \(a\) is a positive integer and whose convergence or divergence is analogous to those of \(\displaystyle \sum a_n\). When \(p > 1\), the improper integral can be evaluated as $$ \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \frac{1}{1-p} x^{1-p} \Big]_{a}^{\beta} = \frac{1}{1-p} \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \Big( \frac{1}{{\beta}^{p-1}} - \frac{1}{a^{p-1}} \Big) $$ $$ = -\frac{1}{1-p}\Big(\frac{1}{a^{p-1}}\Big) $$ $$ \implies \textrm{converges}. $$

We've now proven that a \(p\)-series with \(p > 1\) will converge because the improper integral converges. Now let's consider the case where \(p \lt 1\). Again we'll be looking at the improper integral to determine convergence or divergence: $$ \int_{a}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \frac{1}{1-p} \lim_{\beta \to \infty} x^{1-p} \Big]_{a}^{\beta} = \frac{1}{1-p} \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \Big( \beta^{1-p} - a^{1-p} \Big) $$ $$ \implies \textrm{diverges}. $$

We have one more scenario to consider: when \(p = 1\). $$ \int_{a}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \int_{a}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x} \, \textrm{d}x = \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \ln{(x)} \Big]_{a}^{\beta} $$ $$ = \lim_{\beta \to \infty} \ln{\Big( \frac{\beta}{a} \Big)} $$ $$ \implies \textrm{diverges}. $$

We've now proven the conditions of the \(p\)-series—that when \(p \leq 1\) the series diverges and when \(p > 1 \) the series converges.
