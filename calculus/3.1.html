3.1 — Mean Value Theorem and Rolle's Theorem

In this section, we introduce the Mean Value Theorem and Rolle's Theorem. Recall the definitions of the average rate of change and the instantaneous rate of change. The average rate of change of the function \(f\) over the interval \([a, b]\) is \[\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \, .\] This quantity is the slope of the secant line of the graph of \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b.\) (See Figure 1A.) In contrast, the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at \(x = c\) is \[f'(c) \, ,\] the slope of the tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = c.\) (See Figure 1B.)

Now consider a function \(f\) that is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b).\) If \(f(a) = f(b),\) then the average rate of change of \(f\) over \([a, b]\) is \(0.\) Then, by Rolle's Theorem, there must be a number \(c\) such that \[a \lt c \lt b \and f'(c) = 0 \, .\]

ROLLE'S THEOREM
Let \(f\) be a function that satisfies the following three conditions:
  1. \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b].\)
  2. \(f\) is differentiable on \((a, b).\)
  3. \(f(a) = f(b).\)
Rolle's Theorem then guarantees a number \(c,\) \(a \lt c \lt b,\) such that \(f'(c) = 0.\)

See Figure 2, in which the graph of a function \(y = f(x)\) is shown. \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b),\) therefore satisfying conditions (1) and (2). Moreover, \(f(a) = f(b) = k,\) so \(f\) meets condition (3). Thus, Rolle's theorem guarantees a number c, \(a \lt c \lt b,\) for which \(f'(c) = 0.\) At \(x = c,\) the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a horizontal tangent. Notice that this tangent line is parallel to the secant line formed by the points \((a, k)\) and \((b, k).\)

EXAMPLE 1
Let \(f\) be the function defined by \(f(x) = \frac{1}{3}x^3 - 2x^2 + 2.\) Note that \(f(0) = 2\) and \(f(6) = 2.\) Find the value of \(c\) that satisfies Rolle's Theorem for \(f\) on \([0, 6].\)

Because \(f(x)\) is a polynomial, it is continuous and differentiable for all \(x.\) Moreover, \(f(0) = f(6)\) \(= 2.\) Therefore, \(f\) meets all three conditions of Rolle's Theorem, so there is a guaranteed number \(c\) in \((0, 6)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0\) (Figure 3). Differentiating \(f\) gives \[f'(x) = x^2 - 4x \, .\] We then have \[ \ba f'(c) &= c^2 - 4c = 0 \implies \\ c &= 0 \and c = 4 \, \ea \] The solution in \((0, 6)\) is \(\boxed{c = 4,}\) so this value satisfies Rolle's Theorem for \(f\) on \([0, 6].\)

Let's now discuss the Mean Value Theorem. Suppose that a function \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b).\) The average rate of change of \(f(x)\) over \(a \leq x \leq b\) is \[\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \, . \] The Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \, .\]

Geometrically, the Mean Value Theorem states that the tangent line to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = c\) is parallel to the secant line formed by the points \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b)).\) (See Figure 4.) Thus, Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem, for a horizontal tangent. Additionally, there may be several values of \(c\) in \((a, b)\) that satisfy the Mean Value Theorem. There may be several tangent lines to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) that are parallel to the secant line formed by \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b)).\)

MEAN VALUE THEOREM
If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b),\) then the Mean Value Theorem guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \, .\]
EXAMPLE 2
Let \(g\) be the function defined by \(g(x) = \frac{4}{3}x^3 + 6x^2 - 2x + 1.\) Find all values \(c\) that satisfy the Mean Value Theorem for \(g\) on \([0, 3].\) (Calculator required.)

The function \(g(x)\) is continuous on \([0, 3]\) and differentiable on \((0, 3),\) thereby satisfying the conditions of the Mean Value Theorem on \([0, 3].\) We note that \[g(0) = 1 \and g(3) = 85 \, \, ,\] so the average rate of change of \(g\) on \([0, 3]\) is \[\frac{g(3) - g(0)}{3 - 0} = 28 \, .\] The Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((0, 3)\) such that \[g'(c) = 28 \, .\] We then have \[ \ba g'(c) = 4c^2 + 12c - 2 &= 28\\ 4c^2 + 12c - 30 &= 0 \, , \ea \] whose only solution in \((0, 3)\) is \(\boxed{c = 1.622.}\) Thus, this value satisfies the Mean Value Theorem for \(g\) on \([0, 3].\)

An application of the Mean Value Theorem is convicting speeding drivers. For example, a driver who travels \(180\) miles in two hours has an average speed \[\overline{v} = \frac{180 \; \textrm{miles}}{2 \; \textrm{hrs}} = 90 \; \textrm{mph} \, .\] The driver's position function is continuous and differentiable. Therefore, the Mean Value Theorem guarantees an instant in his journey during which his instantaneous speed is \( \overline{v} = 90\) mph.

EXAMPLE 3
Rainwater accumulates in a container at a rate given by the differentiable function \(R(t),\) where \(R(t)\) is measured in milliliters and \(t\) is measured in minutes. Selected values of \(R(t)\) are shown in the table below for \(0 \leq t \leq 12.\)
\(t\) \(1\) \(3\) \(6\) \(7\) \(8\) \(10\) \(12\)
\(R(t)\) \(1\) \(1.4\) \(1.9\) \(2.4\) \(3.1\) \(3.9\) \(5\)

The average rate of change of \(R(t)\) over \(0 \leq t \leq 12\) is \[ \ba \frac{R(12) - R(0)}{12 - 0} &= \frac{5 - 1}{12} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \; \textrm{mL}/\textrm{min} \, . \ea \] \(R(t)\) is continuous for \(0 \leq t \leq 12\) and differentiable for \(0 \lt t \lt 12.\) The Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((0, 12)\) such that \[R'(c) = \frac{1}{3} \; \textrm{mL}/\textrm{min} \, .\] Therefore, there is a time at which the water in the container changes at a rate of \(\frac{1}{3}\) mL/min.

EXAMPLE 4

Consider Figure 6, in which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is shown. The graph consists of a line segment for \(-6 \lt x \lt 0\) and a semicircle for \(0 \lt x \lt 6.\)
  1. Find the average rate of change of \(f(x)\) over \(0 \leq x \leq 4.\)
  2. Must there be a value \(c,\) \(0 \lt c \lt 4,\) such that \(f'(c)\) equals the average rate of change found in part (a) ?
  3. Find the average rate of change of \(f(x)\) over \(-4 \leq x\) \(\leq 4.\) Does \(f(x)\) satisfy Rolle's Theorem for \(-4 \leq x \leq 4 \; ?\) Explain your reasoning.

  1. We have \(f(0) = 4\) and \(f(4) = 0.\) Thus, the average rate of change is \[\frac{f(4) - f(0)}{4 - 0} = \boxed{-1 \per}\]
  2. \(f\) is continuous on \([0, 4]\) and differentiable on \((0, 4).\) Therefore, the Mean Value Theorem guarantees a value \(c\) in \((0, 4)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(4) - f(0)}{4 - 0} = -1 \per\]
  3. The average rate of change of \(f\) on \([-4, 4]\) is \[\frac{f(4) - f(-4)}{4 - (-4)} = \boxed{0 \per}\] However, \(f\) does not meet the conditions of Rolle's Theorem because \(f(x)\) isn't differentiable over \(-4 \lt x \lt 4.\) (\(f\) is not differentiable at \(x = 0 .\))
SECTION SUMMARY
  • Consider a function \(f\) that is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b).\)
    • The Mean Value Theorem guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \, .\]
    • If \(f(a) = f(b),\) then Rolle's Theorem guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0.\) Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem for \(\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} = 0.\)
  • The Mean Value Theorem guarantees at least one point in \(a \lt x \lt b\) such that the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is parallel to the secant line formed by \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b)).\)
