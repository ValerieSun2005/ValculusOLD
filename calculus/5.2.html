5.2 — Trigonometric Integrals

In this section, we will look discuss how to integrate trigonometric integrals. Before we begin, however, we need to review a table of fundamental integrals of trigonometric functions.

INTEGRALS OF TRIGONOMETRIC FUNCTIONS

\(\displaystyle \int \sin(x) \, \textrm{d}x = -\cos(x) + C \)

\(\displaystyle \int \cos(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \sin(x) + C \)

\(\displaystyle \int \tan(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \ln{|\sec(x)|} + C \)

\(\displaystyle \int \csc(x) \, \textrm{d}x = -\ln{|\csc(x) + \cot(x)|} + C \)

\(\displaystyle \int \sec(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \ln{|\sec(x) + \tan(x)|} + C \)

\(\displaystyle \int \cot(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \ln{|\sin(x)|} + C \)

Let us begin with integrals in the following forms: $$ \int \sin^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We know that an integral in the form \(\displaystyle \int \sin^n(x) \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) can be evaluated with a simple substitution of \(u = \sin(x)\). Similarly, an integral in the form \(\displaystyle \int \cos^n(x) \sin(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) can be evaluated with a simple substitution of \(u = \cos(x)\).

Using these principles, let us take a look at the following example integral: $$ \int \cos^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

Using the Pythagorean identity, we can rewrite the integral as $$ \int \cos(x) \cos^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \cos(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((2)\)

We will now perform the substitution \(u = \sin(x)\) to get $$ u = \sin(x) \implies \textrm{d} u = \cos(x) \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \cos(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \cancel{\cos(x)} \left(1 - u^2 \right) \, \Big(\frac{1}{\cancel{\cos(x)}} \Big) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \int \left(1 - u^2\right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = u - \frac{u^3}{3} + C. $$

\((3)\)

Substituting \(\sin(x)\) back in for \(u\) yields $$ \sin(x) - \frac{\sin^3(x)}{3} + C. $$

\((4)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

INTEGRATING \(\displaystyle \int \sin^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) and \(\displaystyle \int \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\):
For sufficiently large, odd \(n\): $$ \int \sin^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \int \sin(x) \left(1 - \cos^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(\displaystyle u = \cos(x)\).

$$ \int \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x = \int \cos(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(\displaystyle u = \sin(x)\).

For even \(n\): Apply power-reduction formulas, as appropriate. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 1

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \sin^5(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

The integral can be rewritten as $$ \int \sin(x) \sin^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

From here, we can use the Pythagorean identity to further rewrite it as $$ \int \sin(x) \left(1 - \cos^2(x)\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((2)\)

Now, we can perform the substitution \(u = \cos(x)\). $$ \textrm{Let} \, u = \cos(x) $$ $$ \textrm{d} u = - \sin(x) \textrm{d} x. $$ $$ \int \cancel{\sin(x)} \left(1 - u^2\right)^2 \Big(\frac{-1}{\cancel{\sin(x)}}\Big) \, \textrm{d}u $$ $$ = - \int \left(1 - 2u^2 + u^4 \right) \, \textrm{d} u$$ $$ = -\frac{u^5}{5} + \frac{2u^3}{3} - u +C $$

\((3)\)

Substituting back, we obtain $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{- \frac{\cos^5(x)}{5} + \frac{2 \cos^3(x)}{3} - \cos(x) + C.} $$

\((4)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Now let us consider the case in which \(n\) is even. For these cases, it is not as easy—we cannot perform a convenient substitution, as you may verify. Instead, our strategy, in this case, will be to utilize reduction formulas. We know, from the double-angle identity for cosine, that $$ \cos(2x) = \cos^2(x) - \sin^2(x). $$ Performing some algebra and utilizing the Pythagorean identity, we obtain $$ \cos(2x) = \cos^2(x) - \left(1 - \cos^2(x) \right)$$ $$ \cos(2x) = 2 \cos^2(x) - 1 $$ $$ \therefore \cos^2(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \cos(2x) \right). $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$ We can also derive a reduction formula for \(\sin^2(x)\) by performing the same algebraic and trigonometric manipulation. $$ \cos(2x) = \cos^2(x) - \sin^2(x). $$ Using the Pythagorean identity, $$ \cos(2x) = \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right) - \sin^2(x) $$ $$ \cos(2x) = 1 - 2 \sin^2(x) $$ $$ \therefore \sin^2(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left(1 - \cos(2x)\right).$$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

These reduction formulas permit us to simplify integrals involving trigonometric functions of even powers. For example, using the power-reduction formula, we can rewrite $$ \int \cos^2(x) \, \textrm{d}x $$ as $$ \int \frac{1}{2} \left(1 + \cos(2x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which can be easily evaluated with a substitution. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 2

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \cos^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

We will rewrite the integral as $$ \int \left(\cos^2(x)\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} x $$ so that we can apply the power-reduction formula for cosine to get $$ \int \left(\frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \cos(2x) \right)\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

Expanding, we obtain $$ \frac{1}{4} \int \left(1 + 2 \cos(2x) + \cos^2(2x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We now need to apply the power-reduction formula a second time to obtain $$ \cos^2(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \cos(2x) \right), $$ $$ \therefore \cos^2(2x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \cos(4x) \right).$$

\((2)\)

Substituting gives $$ \frac{1}{4} \int \left(1 + 2 \cos(2x) + \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos(4x)) \right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \frac{1}{4} \int \left(\frac{3}{2} + 2 \cos(2x) + \frac{1}{2} \cos(4x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{3}{8}x + \frac{1}{4} \sin(2x) + \frac{1}{32} \sin(4x) + C.} $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Now let us analyze the family of integrals $$ \int \sin^m(x) \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive constants. If \(m\) is odd, then the integral can be rewritten as $$ \int \sin(x) \sin^{m - 1}(x) \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x $$ $$ = \int \sin(x) \left(1 - \cos^2(x)\right)^{\frac{m - 1}{2}} \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ for which we can perform the substitution \(u = \cos(x)\) and convert the integral into a polynomial expression. On the other hand, if \(m\) is even and \(n\) is odd, then the integral can be rewritten as $$ \int \sin^m(x) \cos(x) \cos^{n-1}(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sin^m(x) \cos(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which we can evaluate using the substitution \(u = \sin(x)\). However, if \(m\) and \(n\) are both even, then the integral can be rewritten as $$ \int \left(1 - \cos^2(x)\right)^{\frac{m}{2}} \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which can be expanded and simplified using power-reduction formulas, as appropriate.

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
Example 3

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \sin^2(x) \cos^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

We can rewrite the integral, using the Pythagorean identity, as $$ \int \sin^2(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right) \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

We can now perform the substitution \(u = \sin(x)\) because we have an extra \(\cos(x)\) term in the integrand. $$ u = \sin(x) $$ $$ \textrm{d} u = \cos(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Thus, we obtain $$ \int u^2 \left(1 - u^2 \right) \cancel{\cos(x)} \Big(\frac{1}{\cancel{\cos(x)}}\Big) \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \int \left(u^2 - u^4\right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \frac{u^3}{3} - \frac{u^5}{5} + C. $$

\((2)\)

Substituting back gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{\sin^3(x)}{3} - \frac{\sin^5(x)}{5} + C.} $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Let us now discuss integrals involving other trigonometric functions—those consisting of (1) tangent and secant and (2) cotangent and cosecant. The key strategy for evaluating these integrals involves utilizing the Pythagorean identity. We will be using these forms of the identity: $$ \tan^2(x) + 1 = \sec^2(x) $$ $$ \cot^2(x) + 1 = \csc^2(x). $$

For example, consider the integral $$ \int \sec^4(x) \tan^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Using the Pythagorean identity, we can rewrite the integrand as $$ \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x)\right) \tan^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

Now, we can perform the substitution \(u = \tan(x)\). We know that \(\textrm{d} u = \sec^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), which is helpful in our case because we have a spare \(\sec^2(x)\) factor lying around. We will then get, after performing the substitution, $$ \int \left(1 + u^2\right) \cdot u^3 \, \textrm{d} u. $$

\((2)\)

Expanding, we obtain $$ \int \left(u^3 + u^5\right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \frac{\tan^4(x)}{4} + \frac{\tan^6(x)}{6} + C. $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

To generalize this procedure, let us consider the family of integrals $$\int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive constants.

For sufficiently large, even \(m\), $$ \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x) \sec^{m - 2}(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x) \right)^{\frac{m - 2}{2}} \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which can be evaluated using the substitution \(u = \tan(x)\) to strip the \(\sec^2(x)\) term.

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

For sufficiently large, odd \(n\), $$ \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec(x) \sec^{m - 1}(x) \tan(x) \tan^{n - 1}(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec(x) \tan(x) \sec^{m - 1}(x) \left(\sec^2(x) - 1 \right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x, $$ which can be evaluated using the substitution \(u = \sec(x)\) to strip the \(\sec(x) \tan(x)\).

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$
INTEGRATING \(\displaystyle \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x: \)
For sufficiently large, even \(m\): $$ \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x) \right)^{\frac{m - 2}{2}} \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \tan(x)\).

For sufficiently large, odd \(n\): $$ \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec(x) \tan(x) \sec^{m - 1}(x) \left(\sec^2(x) - 1 \right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \sec(x)\).

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 4

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \sec^4(x) \tan^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Using the Pythagorean identity, the integral can be rewritten as $$ \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x)\right) \tan^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

We can now perform the substitution \(u = \tan(x)\) to account for the \(\sec^2(x)\) in the integral: $$ u = \tan(x) $$ $$ \textrm{d} u = \sec^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We then have $$ \int \cancel{\sec^2(x)} \left(1 + u^2 \right) u^4 \Big(\frac{1}{\cancel{\sec^2(x)}}\Big) \textrm{d} u $$ $$ \int \left(u^4 + u^6 \right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} + \frac{u^7}{7} + C. $$

\((2)\)

Substituting back gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{1}{5} \tan^5(x) + \frac{1}{7} \tan^7(x) + C.} $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

You may verify that the same procedures that we used for integrating \(\displaystyle \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x\) also apply to the family of integrals $$ \int \csc^m(x) \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive integers; that is, consider the following table.

INTEGRATING \(\displaystyle \int \csc^m(x) \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x: \)
For sufficiently large, even \(m\): $$ \int \csc^m(x) \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \csc^2(x) \left(1 + \cot^2(x) \right)^{\frac{m - 2}{2}} \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \cot(x)\).

For sufficiently large, odd \(n\): $$ \int \csc^m(x) \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \csc(x) \cot(x) \csc^{m - 1}(x) \left(\csc^2(x) - 1 \right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \csc(x)\).

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 5

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \csc^{8}(x) \cot^5(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

We start by rewriting, as recommended in the table of procedures, the integral as $$ \int \csc^2(x) \left(1 + \cot^2(x)\right)^3 \cot^5(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

At this point we will perform the substitution \(u = \cot(x)\) to eliminate the \(\csc^2(x)\) in the beginning of the integral: $$ u = \cot(x) $$ $$ \textrm{d} u = -\csc^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We then have $$ \int \cancel{\csc^2(x)} \left(1 + u^2 \right)^3 u^5 \Big(\frac{-1}{\cancel{\csc^2(x)}}\Big) \textrm{d} u $$ $$ -\int \left(u^{11} + 3u^9 + 3u^7 + u^5 \right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = -\frac{u^{12}}{12} - \frac{3u^{10}}{10} - \frac{3u^8}{8} - \frac{u^6}{6} + C. $$

\((2)\)

Substituting back gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{-\frac{1}{12} \cot^{12}(x) - \frac{3}{10} \cot^{10}(x) - \frac{3}{8} \cot^8(x) - \frac{1}{6} \cot^6(x) + C.} $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

For integrals in the form \(\displaystyle \sec^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), where \(n\) is a positive, even integer, they can be rewritten as $$ \int \sec^2(x) \sec^{n - 2}(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x, $$ for which the substitution \(u = \tan(x)\) will strip the \(\sec^2(x)\) factor. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Similarly, for integrals in the form \(\displaystyle \csc^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), where \(n\) is a positive, even integer, they can be rewritten as $$ \int \csc^2(x) \csc^{n - 2}(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \csc^2(x) \left(1 + \cot^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x $$ and evaluated using the substitution \(u = \cot(x)\) to address the \(\csc^2(x)\) factor. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

However, for odd \(n\), it is not as simple. These integrals cannot be evaluated using a clean substitution, so we will mainly use integration by parts to handle these cases. Consider, for example, the integral of \(\displaystyle \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x\). We will integrate by parts by rewriting the expression as $$ \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \sec(x) \sec^2(x) \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

We will choose to differentiate \(\sec(x)\) and integrate \(\sec^2(x)\). Thus, using the integration by parts formula, $$ \int f(x) g'(x) \, \textrm{d} x = f(x) g(x) - \int f'(x) g(x) \, \textrm{d} x, $$ we let \(f(x) = \sec(x)\) and \(g'(x) = \sec^2(x) \). Then, \(f'(x) = \sec(x) \tan(x)\) and \(g(x) = \tan(x)\). Thus, $$ \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \sec(x) \tan(x) - \int \sec(x) \tan^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((2)\)

Using the Pythagorean identity, we can rewrite \(\tan^2(x)\) as \(\sec^2(x) - 1\). We then get $$ \sec(x) \tan(x) - \int \sec(x) \left(\sec^2(x) - 1\right) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \sec(x) \tan(x) - \int \left(\sec^3(x) - \sec(x) \right) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ $$ \therefore \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \sec(x) \tan(x) - \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x + \int \sec(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$

\((3)\)

Adding \(\displaystyle \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) to both sides of the equation yields $$ 2 \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \sec(x) \tan(x) + \int \sec(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \frac{1}{2} \left[\sec(x) \tan(x) + \int \sec(x) \, \textrm{d} x \right]. $$

\((4)\)

We know that \(\int \sec(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| \), so we get $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\int \sec^3(x) \, \textrm{d} x = \frac{1}{2} \Big[\sec(x) \tan(x) + \ln \left|\sec(x) + \tan(x) \right| \Big] + C.} $$

\((5)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

INTEGRATING \(\displaystyle \int \sec^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x\) AND \(\displaystyle \int \csc^n(x) \, \textrm{d}x\):
For sufficiently large, even \(n\): $$ \int \sec^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \tan(x)\).

For sufficiently large, even \(n\): $$ \int \csc^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \csc^2(x) \left(1 + \cot^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Let \(u = \cot(x)\).

For odd \(n\): Attempt to integrate by parts, as appropriate. $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 6

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \sec^6(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

We can rewrite the integral as $$ \int \sec^2(x) \sec^4(x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \sec^2(x) \left(\tan^2(x) + 1\right)^2 \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

At this point we will perform the substitution \(u = \tan(x)\) to eliminate the \(\sec^2(x)\) in the beginning of the integral: $$ u = \tan(x) $$ $$ \textrm{d} u = \sec^2(x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ We then have $$ \int \cancel{\sec^2(x)} \left(u^2 + 1 \right)^2 \Big(\frac{1}{\cancel{\sec^2(x)}}\Big) \textrm{d} u $$ $$ \int \left(u^4 + 2u^2 + 1\right) \, \textrm{d} u $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} + \frac{2u^3}{3} + u + C. $$

\((2)\)

Substituting back gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{1}{5} \tan^5(x) + \frac{2}{3} \tan^3(x) + \tan(x) + C.} $$

\((3)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

The next families of integrals that we will cover involve the forms $$ \int \sin(m x) \sin(n x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \cos(m x) \cos(n x) \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \sin(m x) \cos(n x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$ Recall the following sum-to-product trigonometric identities: $$ \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta) = \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(\alpha - \beta) - \cos(\alpha + \beta)\right] $$ $$ \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) = \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(\alpha + \beta) + \cos(\alpha - \beta)\right] $$ $$ \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) = \frac{1}{2} \left[\sin(\alpha + \beta) + \sin(\alpha - \beta)\right] $$

INTEGRATING PRODUCTS OF SINUSOIDAL FUNCTIONS
$$ \int \sin(m x)\sin(n x) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(mx - nx) - \cos(mx + nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \cos(m x)\cos(n x) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(mx + nx) + \cos(mx - nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ \int \sin(m x)\cos(n x) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\sin(mx + nx) + \sin(mx - nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Example 7

Evaluate the following integral: $$ \int \sin(8x)\sin(4x) \, \textrm{d} x. $$

Using the table of identities mentioned before, we rewrite the integrand as $$ \int \frac{1}{2}\left[\cos(8x - 4x) - \cos(8x + 4x) \right] \, \textrm{d} x $$ $$ = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(4x) - \cos(12x) \right] \, \textrm{d} x. $$

\((1)\)

Evaluating gives $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{\frac{1}{8} \sin(4x) - \frac{1}{24} \sin(12x) + C.} $$

\((2)\)

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

SECTION SUMMARY

  • For positive, odd, sufficiently large \(n\), \(\displaystyle \int \sin^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) and \(\displaystyle \int \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) can be written as \( \displaystyle \int\sin(x) \left(1 - \cos^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x \) and \( \displaystyle \int \cos(x) \left(1 - \sin^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 1}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x \), respectively. The substitutions \(u = \cos(x)\) and \(u = \sin(x)\) should be performed to evaluate each respective integral.
  • For positive, even, sufficiently large \(n\), \(\displaystyle \int \sin^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) and \(\displaystyle \int \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) should be evaluated using the power-reduction formulas \(\displaystyle \sin^2(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left[1 - \cos(2x)\right]\) and \(\displaystyle \cos^2(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left[1 + \cos(2x)\right]\), as appropriate.
  • For the family of integrals \(\displaystyle \int \sin^m(x) \cos^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive constants, try to rewrite the integrand using the Pythagorean identity, \(\sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) = 1\). Utilize the fact that \(\sin'(x) = \cos(x)\) and \(\cos'(x) = -\sin(x)\) to perform the appropriate substitution.
  • For the family of integrals \(\displaystyle \int \sec^m(x) \tan^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive constants, try to rewrite the integrand using the form of the Pythagorean identity \(\tan^2(x) + 1 = \sec^2(x)\). Utilize the fact that \(\sec'(x) = \sec(x) \tan(x)\) and \(\tan'(x) = \sec^2(x)\) to perform the appropriate substitution.
  • For the family of integrals \(\displaystyle \int \csc^m(x) \cot^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\), where \(m\) and \(n\) are positive constants, try to rewrite the integrand using the form of the Pythagorean identity \(\cot^2(x) + 1 = \csc^2(x)\). Utilize the fact that \(\csc'(x) = - \csc(x) \cot(x)\) and \(\cot'(x) = -\csc^2(x)\) to perform the appropriate substitution.
  • For positive, even, sufficiently large \(n\), \(\displaystyle \int \sec^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) and \(\displaystyle \int \csc^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) can be written as \( \displaystyle \int \sec^2(x) \left(1 + \tan^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x \) and \( \displaystyle \int \csc^2(x) \left(1 + \cot^2(x)\right)^{\frac{n - 2}{2}} \, \textrm{d} x \), respectively. The substitutions \(u = \tan(x)\) and \(u = \cot(x)\) should be performed to evaluate each respective integral.
  • For positive, odd, sufficiently large \(n\), evaluating \(\displaystyle \int \sec^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x \) and \(\displaystyle \int \csc^n(x) \, \textrm{d} x\) may require integration by parts, as appropriate.

  • \(\displaystyle \int \sin(mx)\sin(nx) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(mx - nx) - \cos(mx + nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x \).

  • \(\displaystyle \int \cos(mx)\cos(nx) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\cos(mx + nx) + \cos(mx - nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x \).

  • \(\displaystyle \int \sin(mx)\cos(nx) \, \textrm{d} x = \int \frac{1}{2} \left[\sin(mx + nx) + \sin(mx - nx)\right] \, \textrm{d} x \).

  • These formulas should be considered strategies and guidelines for integration, not equations to memorize!
