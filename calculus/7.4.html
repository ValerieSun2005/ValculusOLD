7.4 — Separation of Variables

In this section we will discuss the most-elementary method for solving differential equations—separation of variables. Separation of variables can be used to solve SOME differential equations in the form $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y), $$ when the equation can be re-expressed in the form $$ g(y)\, dy = h(x) \, dx, $$ where we have "separated" the \(x\)- and \(y\)-terms and isolated them on different sides. Our goal with solving any differential equation with this method is to get all of the \(x\)'s on one side and all of the \(y\)'s on the other; if this is not possible, the procedure will not work. Let us now examine a few examples to understand the procedure.

Example 1

Solve the following differential equation, given that \(y(0) = 1\): $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = x+2. $$

For this problem let's rewrite the differential equation as $$ dy = (x+2) \, dx, $$ and then integrating both sides gives $$ \int dy = \int (x+2) \, dx $$ $$ y + C_1 = \frac{x^2}{2} + 2x + C_2. $$ Notice that we have constants of integration from integrating both sides. It is often easier to express them as a single constant, which we will denote \(C\): $$ y = \frac{x^2}{2} + 2x + C. $$ Plugging in the initial condition gives $$ 1 = \frac{(0)^2}{2} + 2(0) + C $$ $$ \Rightarrow C = 1, $$ which we can then plug back into the above equation to obtain $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{y = \frac{x^2}{2} + 2x + 1.} $$

Example 2

Solve the following differential equation, given that \(y(1) = 5\): $$ y \, ' = \frac{2}{x+2}. $$

For this problem let's rewrite the differential equation as $$ dy = \frac{2}{x+2} \, dx, $$ and then integrating both sides gives $$ \int dy = \int \frac{2}{x+2} \, dx $$ $$ y + C_1 = 2\ln{|x+2|} + C_2. $$ Notice that we have constants of integration from integrating both sides. It's often easier to express them as a single constant, which we will denote \(C\): $$ y = 2\ln{|x+2|} + C. $$ Now we plug in the initial condition to solve for \(C\): $$ 5 = 2\ln{|1+2|} + C $$ $$ \Rightarrow C = 5 - 2\ln(3), $$ which we can then plug back into the equation above to obtain $$ y = 2\ln{|x+2|} + (5 - 2\ln(3)) $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{5 + 2\ln{\frac{|x+2|}{3}}.} $$

Example 3

Bacteria in a petri dish grow at a rate modeled by the function \(g(t) = 1 + e^{-2t} \). At \(t = 0\) there are \(100\) bacteria in the dish. Model a function for the amount of bacteria in the petri dish at a given time \(t\).

For simplicity and easy reference let \(B(t)\) denote the number of bacteria in the petri dish at a given time \(t\). We see that \(B'(t) = g(t) = 1 + e^{-2t}\). Rewriting this, we obtain $$ \frac{dB}{dt} = 1 + e^{-2t}, $$ and we can now multiply by both sides by \(dt\) to get $$ dB = (1 + e^{-2t}) \, dt. $$ Integrating, we get $$ \int dB = \int (1 + e^{-2t}) \, dt $$ $$ B(t) = t -\frac{1}{2} e^{-2t} + C. $$ We know that at \(t = 0, B(t) = 100\), so we can use this information to find \(C\): $$ 100 = 0 - \frac{1}{2}e^{-2(0)} + C $$ $$ \Rightarrow C = \frac{201}{2}. $$ Plugging back in for \(B(t)\) gives us our function of $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{B(t) = t - \frac{1}{2}e^{-2t} + \frac{201}{2}.} $$
