2.4 — The Chain Rule

Up to this point, we have seen the power, product, and quotient rules. However, the one form we have yet to discuss is the derivative of a composite function, \(f(g(x))\). After this section, we will be able to differentiate every single-variable function using a combination of the rules that we have learned so far.

Suppose that we were given the function \(\sin(2x)\) and sought to differentiate it. How would we do so? We cannot simply write "\(\cos(2x)\)," as we are working with a composite function and therefore require a new tool—the chain rule. Suppose that we have the composite function \(y = f(g(x))\). If we interpret the rate of change of \(y\) as \(y'\), then we notice that the rate of change depends (1) on the outer function, \(f\), and (2) on the inner function, \(g\). In our case, the rate of change of \(\sin(2x)\) is affected by the inner function, \(2x\). We know, analytically, that the coefficient of \(2\) doubles the frequency of the parent function \(\sin(x)\) and thus doubles the rate of change. Therefore, it is logical to say that $$ \sin'(2x) = \cos(2x) \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x}(2x) = 2 \cos(2x). $$ This rule can be generalized by following form:

CHAIN RULE
$$ \Big(f(g(x))\Big)' = f'(g(x)) g'(x). $$ Alternatively, written in Leibniz notation, let \(u = g(x)\). Then $$ \frac{\textrm{d}y}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{\textrm{d}y}{\textrm{d}u} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}u}{\textrm{d}x}, $$ where \(y = f(g(x))\).

Written in words, the chain rule instructs us to first differentiate the outer function with respect to the inner function. That is, let \(u\) be the inner function, and differentiate the outer function with respect to \(u\). Then, differentiate the inner function \(u\) with respect to \(x\). In the case of \(\sin(2x)\), we let \(u = 2x\). Then, $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x}[\sin(2x)] = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} u}[\sin(u)] \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[u] $$ $$ = \cos(u) \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[2x]$$ $$ = 2 \cos(2x). $$

Example 1

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} [\cos(x^2)]\).

For this problem, let \(u = x^2\). Then $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} [\cos(x^2)] = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} u}[\cos(u)] \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[u]$$ $$ -\sin(u) \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[x^2] $$ $$ -\sin(x^2) \cdot 2x $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{-2x\sin(x^2).}$$

Example 2

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} \left[e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4}\right]\).

For this problem, the inner function is \(u = -x^2 + 3x - 4\). Then $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} [e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4}] = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} u}[e^u] \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[u] $$ $$ e^u \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[-x^2 + 3x - 4] $$ $$ e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4} \cdot (-2x + 3) $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{(3 -2x)e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4}.}$$

Example 3

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} [\sin^3(x)]\).

We should rewrite the function as \( \displaystyle \left(\sin(x)\right)^3\), which will make it clear that the inner function, \(u\), is \(\sin(x)\). Therefore, $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x} \left[ \left(\sin(x)\right)^3 \right] = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} u}[u^3] \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[u] $$ $$ 3u^2 \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d} x}[\sin(x)] $$ $$ 3\sin^2(x) \cdot \cos(x) $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{3 \sin^2(x) \cos(x).} $$

Now we can differentiate various families of functions. Let us start with the derivative of \(b^x\), where \(b\) is a positive constant. Using logarithmic properties, we know that $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x}\left(b^x\right) = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[e^{x\ln(b)} \right]. $$ Letting \(u = x \ln(b)\), we obtain, using the chain rule, $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[e^{x\ln(b)} \right] = \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}u}\left(e^u\right) \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x}(u) $$ $$ = e^{u} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x}\left[{x \ln(b)}\right]$$ $$ = e^{x \ln(b)} \cdot \ln(b) $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{b^x \ln(b).} $$ Applying the same procedure for other functions, we have the derivatives of the following family of functions: $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[\sin(ax) \right] = a \cos(ax). $$ $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[\cos(ax) \right] = -a \sin(ax). $$ $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[e^{ax}\right] = ae^{ax}. $$

Example 4

Find the \(n\)th derivative of \(f(x) = e^{5x} \).

If we write out a few derivatives, we see that $$ f'(x) = 5 e^{5x} $$ $$ f''(x) = 5^2 e^{5x} $$ $$ f'''(x) = 5^3 e^{5x} $$ $$ f^{(4)}(x) = 5^4 e^{5x }$$ $$ \cdots $$ We notice that every additional derivative is multiplied by \(5\). The recursive formula is $$ f^{(n)}(x) = 5f^{(n-1)}(x), $$ and the explicit formula is $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt] {f^{(n)}(x) = 5^n e^x.} $$

Let us now introduce contextual applications to the chain rule. Suppose that the number of birds by a lake depends on how many fish are in the lake. The number of fish in the lake as a function of time is modeled by \(F(t)\), and the number of birds is, as an example, modeled by the composite function \(B(F(t))\). Therefore, the expression for the rate at which the number of birds is changing with respect to time is given by $$ \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}t} = \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}F} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}F}{\textrm{d}t}. $$ The chain rule permits us to break up \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}t}\) into two meaningful and known quantities, \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}F}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}F}{\textrm{d}t}\). We modeled the number of birds by the lake as a function of fish in the lake, and we modeled the number of fish in the lake as a function of time. The chain rule enabled us to combine those functions to determine \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}F}\), which was previously unknown. The easy way to remember the chain rule is to imagine "canceling out," in this case, the \(\textrm{d}F\) terms as if \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}F}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}F}{\textrm{d}t}\) were fractions. However, it is important to remember that \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}B}{\textrm{d}F}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}F}{\textrm{d}t}\) are meaningful expressions that should not actually be considered fractions.

Example 5

The number of people outside, \(N\), is modeled as a function of the temperature on a given day, \(T(t)\), where \(t\) represents hours after 8 AM. At \(t = 3\), the temperature is changing at a rate of \(3.4\) degrees Fahrenheit per hour, and the number of people outside is changing at a rate of \(0.8\) person per degree Fahrenheit. What is the rate of change of the number of people outside with respect to time at \(11\) AM?

From the question, we know that \(N\) is modeled as \(N(T(t))\). We want to find \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}N}{\textrm{d}t}\), and we are given \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}N}{\textrm{d}T}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}T}{\textrm{d}t}\). Thus, by the chain rule, $$ \frac{\textrm{d}N}{\textrm{d}t} = \frac{\textrm{d}N}{\textrm{d}T} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}T}{\textrm{d}t}. $$ $$ \frac{\textrm{d}N}{\textrm{d}t} = \left(0.8 \, \frac{\textrm{person}}{\cancel{^{\circ}\textrm{F}}} \right) \left(3.4 \, \frac{\cancel{^{\circ}\textrm{F}}}{\textrm{hr}}\right) $$ $$ = \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{2.72 \frac{\textrm{person}}{\textrm{hr}}.} $$ At \(11\) AM, the number of people outside is increasing at a rate of \(2.72\) people per hour.

Example 6

In a zoo, it is known that every animal on display brings \(2\) visitors into the zoo. Throughout the day, the number of animals wandering in public display areas is given by \(\displaystyle A(t) = 300 + \frac{104}{\pi}\sin{\left( \frac{\pi}{6} t \right)}\), where \(t\) is measured in hours since \(6\) AM. What is the rate at which the number of visitors is changing with respect to time at \(12\) PM?

For this problem, let \(V\) represent the number of visitors. We need \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}V}{\textrm{d}t}\). In this case, $$ \frac{\textrm{d}V}{\textrm{d}t} = \frac{\textrm{d}V}{\textrm{d}A} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}A}{\textrm{d}t} $$ $$ \frac{\textrm{d}V}{\textrm{d}t}\Big|_{t = 6}= 2 \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t} \left[300 + \frac{104}{\pi}\sin{\left(\frac{\pi}{6} t \right)}\right]_{t = 6}$$ $$ = 2 \cdot \left(\frac{\pi}{6} \right) \frac{104}{\pi} \cos(\pi) $$ $$ = -\frac{104}{3} $$ $$ \approx \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{-34.667.} $$ People are leaving the zoo at a rate of \(34.667\) people per hour at \(12\) PM.

Example 7

Evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[\sin^2(3x^4)\right] \).

For this problem, notice that we need to use the chain rule twice. It may be easier to rewrite the expression as $$ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}x} \left[\left(\sin(3x^4)\right)^2\right]. $$ This problem is in the form \(\displaystyle y = f(g(h(x))),\) where \(\displaystyle h(x) = 3x^4, g(x) = \sin(x),\) and \(\displaystyle f(x) = x^2\). Thus, the derivative is given by $$ \frac{\textrm{d}y}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{\textrm{d}f}{\textrm{d}g} \frac{\textrm{d}g}{\textrm{d}h} \frac{\textrm{d}h}{\textrm{d}x}. $$ We will therefore have $$ f'(g(h(x)) \cdot g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x) $$ $$ 2 \left(\sin(3x^4)\right) \cdot \cos(3x^4) \cdot 12x^3 $$ $$ \bbox[border: 2px solid white, 2pt]{24x^3 \sin(3x^4) \cos(3x^4).}$$

SECTION SUMMARY

  • The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions in the form \(\displaystyle f\left(g(x)\right)\).
  • \(\displaystyle \Big(f\left(g(x)\right)\Big)' = f'(g(x)) g'(x)\).
  • Using Leibniz notation, if \(\displaystyle u = g(x)\) and \(\displaystyle y = f\left(g(x)\right) \), then \(\displaystyle \frac{\textrm{d}y}{\textrm{d}x} = \frac{\textrm{d}y}{\textrm{d}u} \cdot \frac{\textrm{d}u}{\textrm{d}x}\).
  • The chain rule can be used to create a composition of known rates.
